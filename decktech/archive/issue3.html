DeckTech slows down with the rest of the SW: CCG universe

It’s been awhile since the last issue of DeckTech but here it is. This month we emphasize quality over quantity. Yes some people submitted decks and tournament reports, but it is not my intention to make you the subscribers of DeckTech wade through a bunch of mediocre verbiage to find the good stuff. I simply won’t waste your time with it. DeckTech is evolving into a different kind of Newsletter. The Team at DeckTech are not sure what yet, but we are excited to play our part in this online drama.

Expect things to pick up again when Endor is released and as we approach the regionals. For now the excitement of TPM and YJ have all contributed to the overall stagnation of SW: CCG. Endor will jumpstart us and provide us the opportunity to shine as the Internets best online SW: CCG players resource.

Thanks to everyone who contributed this month. Read and Enjoy.

A word or two on the Star Wars Celebration
by Neal Razi

"I'm sorry, I just think that your senses and alters are cowardly." - one of my opponents after an 8 point loss at the celebration tournament. Except for 2 force drains, all his damage came from Uncontrollable Fury and Anger, Fear, Aggression.

Neal here. i'll keep this brief, as i know these can get quite long. Rather than a blow by blow account of games, i'm going to try to focus on the larger picture, as always draw some conclusions and give a special nod to deck tech.

About 60-70 players came, nearly all very high caliber. Number one ranked, numerous top 100 and Matt Potter were all there. Tournament started late, and was only three games, everyone receiving a bye to keep it "sanctioned"( i use quotes because I don't think Decipher ever counts these toward rankings. I haven't seen this one count yet.) Since it was three games only, the winner, who received a full set of Endor, was going to be strictly down to points. I can't even tell you who won, sorry! It didn't matter to me, it wasn't me and i was too busy trying to soak up some real quality tournament experience, which is very hard to come by. Let's move on to the rulings I encountered/overheard, in order of interest, although i'll list all for reference.

1) Secret Plans - Partial force could be spent to partially retrieve force.

Several times in the tournament a light side player couldn't afford to retrieve all force in one shot(all wings report in, mind what you have learned). The player was allowed to just spend a portion of force, retrieve that amount of force and leave the rest canceled. I suspect this may be wrong, is there an "official" ruling?

2) Hidden Base - a minumum of five systems must be deployed not six.

my opponent claimed in his area the errata phrase "and your hidden base" meant in addition to the five battleground systems. he was, of course, wrong.

3) Droid Merchant - One force for each droid merchant per droid

This one has been reported inconsistently. I agreed with the ruling. no i was not playing maelstrom at the celebration(more on this later)

4) Bespin: Cloud City - It is a Cloud City Location.

In response to occupation of Bespin system and deployment costs. this was obvious to me, yet i'm reporting it anyway.

Conclusions Drawn

Feel free to disagree

1) Hidden base is still a very viable deck! Ignore it at your extreme peril.

2) Hidden Base swarm, however, is over-rated and probably less effective than other variants.

3) Sense, Alter can still be extremely effective in the tournament scene.

4) Yarna Gargan, and Well-Guarded are very dangerous starting effects. Many people lost them on the first or second turn, and got grim-nocked out of the game.

5) Despite the rebels being able to just plain whup-up on the empire via a number of ways, there are still many who WILL play a "coward" deck. Be prepared with some kind of insert control, and seriously consider, Come Here You Big Coward. This reverses an earlier feeling of mine.

6) If you go to a tournament, expect to see maelstrom and no shelter(more later), and the isb spread out and drain, ghhk deck.

7) Deck tech is a very useful and highly effective resource.(keep reading)

Deck Tech report

I was very happy to find two other deck tech correspondents at the celebration. John Arendt and Mike Gerard not only seemed to be excellent players, but high caliber persons to match. I have to admit, it was nice to have a feeling of comradery at such a far away tournament, and i found the information exchange principles of deck tech are very useful. All three of us did very well, i was undefeated, John was 2 and 1, don't know about mike's final record, but i did see him crush someone. Furthermore, i'd hazard that over 50% of light side players were playing variants of either editor David Aker's Maelstrom deck or correspondent Mike Fitzgerald's No Shelter, a great tribute to the caliber of players that write here. I heard numerous isb'ers crying how they couldn't ghhk after being hit with Draw Their Fire and Frozen Assets or Beggar, or getting Revolutioned on the first turn. Reading deck tech I have concluded, can gain you an edge in such an "internet deck" dominated scene.

And it's nice to have a friendly face or two at a world caliber tournament!

A final word about the celebration itself

Nothing to do with any tournaments, here it is. The celebration was moderately fun, but if you missed it, don't regret it. The weather was miserable, the staff even worse(security even worse than that!). The most interesting part, for me, was seeing and listening to the actors and their stories, they were actually both very, very gracious(especially Ray Park, a great guy) and quite entertaining(Anthony Daniels was hilarious). The displays in the main area, were overly commercialized, although some were interesting. The fan costumes, TOP NOTCH! Life size X-wing- not even close, Youg Jedi- boring, official store- a three hour line, didn't even go. Overall, fun if you went, but don't feel bad if you didn't.

The Mind Game
by Neal Razi

You are locked in a tight match. You and your opponent are matched in skill, and in deck quality. Who's going to win? Some would say the luckier player, but generally it'll be the first one who slips, and in a tight, tense game say it's the sixth game of a tournament, usually someone cracks. Since there are so many excellent(and i do mean excellent) articles this issue dealing with being a good player, i'm going to talk about an area i've become increasingly interested in lately, the so called "Mind Game".

Believe me you are engaging in this now whether you know it or not, at every tournament. You and your opponents, the better ones at least, are locked in a struggle of wills, as well as cards. A chess national champion(my Fortran teacher) once told me, "At that point, Neal, there's almost never a new move, because all the moves have been done a hundred years ago. Basically it boils down to who can out-pysch the other." Now if you think swccg can get rough or cutthroat, just imagine a national chess tournament! He told me players there will do anything to distract their opponent, even to the extent of wearing glasses crooked just to annoy the opposition. Bobby Fisher, a world chess champion, was well noted for his general rudeness, opponents hated to play against him, he was just so obnoxious. Do you think tennis great John McEnroe cried all the time just because he was angry? No, he was trying to break the concentration of his oppostion. At this point, I'm going to recommend you all go out and rent a great movie, "Pumping Iron". It's a documentary about Body Building in the 70's at it's peak(the great Arnold Schwarzenegger is the main man), and you will be amazed at the lengths others will go to throw your mind. If you are serious about competition, there is a lot to be learned from this movie.

Now at this point, before going into detail, I'd like to state a couple things. Number 1, I am in no way an expert! I am just making conclusions on what I see, and other deck techers, please resond to either David or me on this article. Number 2, I am in no way advocating that when you play, you make a total ass out of yourself, as a matter of fact please don't. There are enough asses in the world today, and I'm not talking about donkeys. I just want you to be prepared and to learn and help me learn from some of the concepts I'm going to talk about. I'm going to talk about the ways of using and countering each strategy, remember i am NOT recommending them, just analyzing.

Ok, let's get down to business. First, I'm going to go through some of the known ways of distracting. This is not an extensive list, like I said email with more. You may also encounter more than one at the same time. Later, I'm going to introduce a concept that only recently occured to me.

1) The cocky attitude - This one I hate most of all, because over the years, through basketball, video game tournaments and swccg, this is the one that gives me the hardest time. The cocky player will continually boast, draw attention to his own "great" moves("Man, did you see what i did, that was beautiful, have you ever seen anything that beautiful?") and try to make you feel dumb. My advice, don't get into an attitude war with him, he is an expert and usually very quick witted, and you'll wind up losing the verbal war, getting frustrated and angry, and slipping. For those of you who wish to try this method, remember, you're upping the ante! If you lose, you'll look very, very stupid. You have to be good, and frankly not mind being a total ass.

2) The outburster - Our friend and editor David Akers often uses this strategy. Suddenly, in a very quiet tense moment, the outburster will start laughing, or singing, or sneezing or use some other kind of sharp, shocking, mind attack to destroy your concentration. My advice, simply laugh, and regroup, wait a second or two, and to get back on your train of thought, look around the table. Psychology has showed that visual clues will help mentally retrace your steps. If you want to try this, it is one of the least obnoxious, and I think used sparingly, just might be enough to swing the game at a crucial point. Plus, it's just passive-aggressive enough to be impossible to prove!

3) The pesterer - The pesterer will keep bugging you about details. Rather than one thunderous attack, he will rely on you thinking about the questions he is continually asking you taking up part of your processing power, leaving you with less brain resources to use in the game itself.

Sadly, I have used this tactic, when I find an opponent annoying. Example, At the celebration(i mentioned this in an earlier article), I came up against a dagobah, force retrieval, training, hide and insert deck, and the dude put my most hated card, Uncontrollable Fury, on me on like the 4th turn. Now in that kind of slow game, U.F. will be your doom. So I was just irritated enough to start pestering. Everyone knows how complicated those jedi tests can be right? Well, i proceeded to ask him to explain in detail every possible ramification, outcome, chance of completing, deployment restriction, can it move, can i cancel it, can you subtract from my destinies for sense and alter if you wanted to(I was particulary proud of that one!), I mean everything, and you know what I could see him having to stop and think about it, and losing concentration. I won by 8 points, and while we were playing such opposite decks that perhaps it didn't have an effect, all it takes in an 8 point game is one crucial move to swing it the other way. Plus, I wanted to punish him for that sort of deck.

I recommend for this, to simply turn the ball over to him, and say "read the card". David will frequently do this to a pestering opponent, or counter by insulting his intelligence ("don't you know any of the rules?" or something)so he wil stop. If you want to try this, it can be somewhat effective and it can be a good "payback" tactic to an annoying opponent.

4) Uncontrollable Fury - You may find yourself against an opponent who starts getting very angry. This will be very unnerving, because it is instinctive nature in nearly all animals to remove themselves from a violently angry creature. They will start slowly, perhaps by whining, and if a turn of events go against them, escalate. At this point they are hoping that sub consciously you will let up, and you know what, you may. I recommend telling yourself, "This is a ploy and if it's not then there is no reason for someone to get this angry over a game." Don't tell them, "To take it easy" they usually will only increase their rage, because they can see it's getting to you. If it gets out of hand, ask the tournament director to remove them from the tournament. If they forfeit a game(40+ differencial) enough times, they'll stop. Try to remember though, sometimes it is unintentional, they may acutally be a decent person who is in some kind of bad circumstance. If you use this strategy, be forewarned, no one will want to play against you, and you run the run the risk of becoming a stagnant player. Also, this may not matter to you, but it is very unsportsmanlike. That being said, unfortunately, i have seen this strategy work.

5) The sloth - Another absolutely infuriating tactic is to just try to drag the game out and make it so boring that the opponent loses interest and hence concentration, begins playing recklessly. Believe me, I have played a player that was so agonizingly slow that I wanted to throw the game. They will sit there and ask dumb questions like "what's your total power at that site?" 5 or 6 times or just keep looking at their cards, or keep taking cards back. This is a hard strategy to combat, and i'm open to suggestions. David recommends calling the director over on stalling, but it is very difficuly to prove. I usually just try to play fast, sort of a shame factor, and use the opponent's turn to just plan my move ahead so i can move quickly. warning, do not play so fast you become reckless, it is very, very easy to do as you will underestimate your opponent. If you play this tactic, you will probably not become a very good player due to the fact you will end up timing out frequently, and once again you will get little practice because no one will want to play against you.

6) Speedy Gonzalez - Strangely, I have encountered this too, a player who deploys at warp speed(and i'm a pretty fast player) in an effort to throw you off. generally, he will end up making an incredible amount of mistakes, but see the strategies for The Sloth and reverse them.

7) The Weirdo - Rarely seen, this harkens back to what I said earlier about crooked glasses. The weirdo will just try to behave so strangely that you will be distracted. I once played against a player at the regionals who periodically moaned and proceeded to play with his arm thorough his neck hole. He beat me, in a 5 point match! I know i made mistakes, and I think I might lost due to his behavior, which was insulting my intelligence, and hence made me angry. I've heard of players wearing whirling beanies at Magic tournaments. I think the best thing to do, once again, is laugh at this thing, acknowledge the weirdness to yourself, and then just give the illusion of ignoring it. Don't let it get to you, and don't complain about it, you'll be playing right into his hands. If you feel confident, say, "Man, you are one weird dude." If you want to try this, go ahead, I am at this point unsure of its effectiveness, all you have to do is think of some crazy looking or sounding gimmik, and just try to unconcern yourself with our "normal" social mores.

8) The Illusion of Ineptness - A sometimes very effective limited use ploy, particularly effective in swccg.

This opponent will usually tell you how bad they are, and make you feel sorry for them, they may even make an intentional small "mistake". Beware this can often be a ruse to lull you into overconfidence. Most of us feel sorry for the new guy, or the guy who has recently started and humbly wants to improve, so we often "take it easy" on them, to be a sportsman. Also, since in swccg, differencial can be important, in a game you feel you have won, so you may stop picking up cards for fear they will be caught in your hand. But when your guard is down, BANG out comes the smack down.

Story: Out local tournament director, Rob, and I were involved in a game, i was still somewhat new to the scene, although i felt i was becoming a strong player. I had the upper hand with my empire deck, and Rob was telling me how much he "sucked", that this is why he always did bad in tournaments, he never got to play enough... . he even missed a force drain or two, in retrospect, perhaps on purpose. I stopped deploying, for he was down to about 15 life force, and I had all his mains locked up at Obi-wans hut, and despite their sabers and profeciencies, my space based imperial decree negated those Bonuses and I was draining from the Jundland wastes and The cantina. Just when i relaxed my guard, Out it came, Nabrun leids to the cantina, where Motti, and another decent character were, i missed sensing it, BOOM, Gift of the Mentor, Don't get Cocky, Courage of a skywalker(luke ended up like power 12), Jedi presence all my guys hit and forfeit 0! I had NEVER seen that before. I threw out almost 40 force, and picked up the rest. In one fell swoop, I was gone. Brilliant.

To guard against this, watch out when someone is telling you they stink, or they make mistakes and lament them. Don't let your mind slip, stay in the game, especially if it looks like a type of deck that could deal out a lot of battle damage. If you want to use this, it can win you a game, but it'll only work once, MAYBE twice against the same player. If things are going against you, you might try this and hope for a home-run. After all what's one force drain or spending a couple extra force, if you deal out 40 points of battle damage. Remember, though, this is not a long term strategy.

9) The Wall - The so called "Pokerface". I'm putting this last, because it's probably the best. This person will show no emotion no matter what happens, not rude, angry or happy. I think many of the top players use this mindset. Consider this, if high stakes poker players, who are playing for a lot more than a t-shirt use this, there has to be merit to it.

Another story: Last years regionals, deck tech correspondent Mike Fitzgerald(he talks about his anti-jp deck in this issues article, and this was the same one) against the previous years champ, Rusty Zion, playing in the title match. Mike was playing anti-JP mains, Rusty playing JP-manipulator. Mike was gaining the upper hand, when he plays a Grimtaash lost on Rusty. Rusty loses about 8 cards(!) Scanning Crews, and Dangerous Times. At this point, I'd have cracked, I'd have said "oh crap", something, but Rusty just puts out the cards and doesn't even look up. Mike, with no emotion, says, "I'd like to see the rest of your hand". It was beautiful, on both parts.

There is generally no counter to this strategy, because to a skilled player of this style, it's nearly impossible to make them crack, they will ignore everything. At least, they generally don't even try to psych you out as well. As for playing this, it's a great way to play, but it takes a LOT of concentration and skill, and it is, to me, mentally taxing as well. If you can pull it off, try it.

Ok, last section is a concept I've been considering recently, and that is the power of duplicity, trickery and "bluffing". Once again, I'm looking to high stakes poker. We all have tried this at one time or another, even if it's asking "How many cards are in your hand?" when there is not a monnok in your deck. I think there is a tremendous amount of game-swaying power here, because often these will almost give you the benefits of extra cards in your deck, such as the afforementioned line. You might play a surprise assault, get an exceptionally good draw, and say "Oh yeah, my deck is all 5 and 6's" when in reality of course it's not. You then leave a force down every turn. It doesn't matter whether you have a surprise assault or not, your opponent won't drain, unless he thinks he can thwart it, or somehow look at your hand. So what I'm looking for I guess are ways you guys have found to outtrick your opponent without just going into a pych routine. I'll start it off, and it's my last story

I was playing a hidden base, and it was coming out unusally fast. Unbeknowst to my opponent, I had already deployed my hidden base, as well as the requisite battlegrounds, but did not flip the objective. Why? I was in no serious force drain danger yet, and i had another system in my hand, let's say "Anoat". So, here you go, i began muttering, drawing cards and breathed a sigh of relief after a couple cards and stopped drawing. My turn rolls around, I promptly deploy "Anoat" from my hand, and flipped the objective. Now where do you think he deployed his first and only starship to to probe. Of course, "Anoat". There's no card in existence which can duplicate this effect! With the power of my trickery, I caused a large squandering of resources.

So wrapping it up, I'm very interested in other specific instances of this sort of thing, or general rules, I think they are even more effective than the psych-out routines mentioned above, as they should work on nearly any player. The sneakier the better. Please respond to David or myself on this article, I'd like to see what kind of followups you have, and we will post them next issue. I further wish to state, I never would have posted this to a general audience, only to deck tech, because of the quality of players I have seen here, and some of the benefits I have already received.

Conclusion: The mind is a terrible thing to waste someone with. It is a potent pychological weapon. Be prepared to maximize yours and counter his.

 

The Ingredients of an Excellent SW: CCG Player.
By David M. Akers

If you want to bake a cake you need to know a few things first. The first thing you need is to know is the ingredients. After you know what ingredients are needed then you need to know what to do with them. By using this simple method you can bake a cake that reasonably resembles the original, again and again. This is what is called duplication.

Duplication is simply the ability to replicate an object or outcome time and time again. Scientist use this concept as their framework for proving theories. In order for something to be considered proven it must be able to be duplicated time and time again.

If your goal is to become a excellent SW: CCG player then there are certain ingredients that you should be aware of that can be found in almost every excellent SW: CCG player. This article list the top 5 ingredients that are found in most excellent SW: CCG players. Study them, analyze them, adapt them, and assimilate them. What ever you do incorporate them into yourself and you to will become an excellent player.

Determination – If when you loose (and you will, everyone does) you get discouraged and sulk or quit, if you use SW: CCG as another way to reinforce your already negative self-image, you will never be an excellent SW: CCG player.

If however your turn your losses into fuel that drives you harder and faster to practice and study, then even in defeat you will become a better player. It doesn’t matter if you win or loose a determined player will evolve. Just remember the goal, it’s not to win a game or a tournament, but to improve! Be determined to improve with every tournament, every game, every win, every loss, every character you deploy, every force you activate. Be determined to become better and you will become an excellent SW: CCG player.

Teachable – If you can not be taught you will never become an excellent SW: CCG player. If you are the only one that can teach you, you will never become an excellent SW: CCG player. You must not only be able to learn from yourself, through your own mistakes, but you must also be able to learn from the mistakes of others and the lessons they have to teach.

There are simply way too many possible mistakes for you to learn them all from experience. Learn from those better than you what you should do. Learn from those worse than you what you should not do. Absorb their knowledge like a sponge. There is no shame in admitting someone else knows more than you do. In fact there is liberation in such genuine humility. Liberation to improve and evolve. Without such humility and teachability, you will grow stagnant as a player, and old, and eventually obsolete.

Know Thyself – If you can not take an objective look and honest look at yourself the SW: CCG player you might not ever reach excellence. Take an honest look at your strengths and weaknesses. See what are your best qualities and play to maximize your strengths and minimize your weaknesses.

Excellent players practice the skills that they are weakest in first, fearful players continually practice their strengths and ignore the weakness. It is their Achilles heel, it is what prevents them from reaching true excellence. To be truly great you must come to grips with your personal weaknesses, acknowledge them, but refuse to let them master you. Master your weaknesses. Everyone has a weakness, the excellent players are aware of theirs and know how to minimize it, in doing so they become able to more clearly see the weakness of others and ultimately exploit them.

Emotional Detachment – Anger, Fear, Hate these are the enemies of an excellent SW: CCG player. To become a truly excellent player you must not let your emotions interfere with your discussions. Master your emotions. Tame them as if they were an dangerous animal that is out of control.

More players have lost to this weakness than from forgetting to force drain. I am not saying excellent players play like robots. They have learned to subdue their emotions and use them as internal energy. They are not flustered or upset, angry or sad. Once you let your emotions cloud your judgement you have already lost.

Sense of Humor – Finally to be able to properly implement any of the above a player must have a sense of humor. Many people will skip over this but this is perhaps the most important of them all. It is what enables the others. Laugh at yourself every once and a while. Laugh at the lone jawa facing down Obi – Wan. Have fun. It is the vent you need to release that excess pressure and it’s a way to find really enjoyable moments in the game. Once and awhile you need to forget about your rating, forget about winning, just concentrate on having fun. Ratings are counterproductive to an excellent player anyway. Sabotage your rating every once and a while and keep yourself honest. But more importantly try to have fun and find moments not be serious at all.

By incorporating these qualities into yourself, you will become an excellent SW: CCG player.

What makes a good tournament player?
By Michael Hawly


Well first let's define our terms. What is meant by a "good tournament player?" Given the focus of the deck tech newsletter, I assume that 'good tournament player' means a player who wins or finishes in the top few players at most of the tournaments he or she enters. While I will focus on the characteristics that help players win, I have to say that winning does not make you a 'good tournament player.' I am tempted to rant about sportsmanship here ... consider yourself ranted at.

There are two general qualities that make a winning tournament player -- preparedness and presence of mind. I'll address preparedness first.

Deck building:
Deck building is the obvious part of preparedness. A winning player is aware of what deck types are available and popular and can adapt a deck to suit his/her own style of play and to respond to the types of decks he/she is likely to play. A really good player will have some element of surprise or uniqueness to his/her decks which make them challenging for even an experienced opponent.

Knowledge of the cards:
Knowledge of the cards gives you the universe of possibilities. Obviously this will help with your deck building. Is there a card that achieves your goal, which is more efficient or has a higher destiny than the one you saw in that deck posting? The better your knowledge of the cards the better your chances of making good choices. It will also give you a better idea of what to worry about during the game.

Knowledge of the rules:
This sounds trivial but we all know that the rules are not simple nor are they intuitive. Often, when I add a new strategy to a deck, I E-mail the tournament director to get an advance ruling and confirm the ruling in
person on the day of the tournament.

Knowledge of local trends:
What decks/cards to people play/fear. What is the local approach to SAC and Grimnok. Local trends are of supreme importance. I play in Kashyyk, which deck-wise is a bit of a backwater. A while back, when the JP Manipulator deck took the world by storm, nobody played it around here. I'm not sure why. But, if you were watching the web sites, you would have built one of the many anti-manipulator decks and been in for a big surprise when you started with the Throne Room and the dark side started with the Death Star.

Next I will address presence of mind. Presence of mind is what makes the difference in a game where the decks are well matched and neither player has overwhelmingly good or bad luck. It keeps a player from losing due to avoidable factors. It expands winning differentials and trims losing differentials.

Adaptability:
The best players can respond to a game that has not unfolded according to plan. They can spot a weakness in the opponents’ deployment and exploit it.

Focus:
I think the most important thing for a tournament player to remember is to be in the game at all times. Concentration is the hallmark of the very best players. A great player will often be able to tell you his/her destiny numbers before drawing them -- and then tell you yours! Good players count their cards and track their destinies. They don't let their opponents’ switch from one phase to another without giving them an opportunity to play a card or perform an action. They don't forget anything.

Tenacity:
It is very important to play each game to the finish. There is something to be learned even in a humiliating defeat. Good players don't concede defeat until defeat is inevitable. Even then they look for ways to minimize the damage. They also continue to size up their opponents’ deck to see if there are ways to counter it that they hadn't thought of. Contrariwise, a great player never relaxes his grip on a worthy opponent. There is always a danger that an opponent will mount an unexpected comeback. That said, (sportsmanship rant warning!!!) it is important not to rub your opponent's face in it. I consider it a sign of respect for my opponent to treat me as potentially dangerous even when I am down. I am an adult and I have confidence in my abilities. A good player gives the newer, younger, or weaker players a break. If they seem receptive, a good player will talk strategy, make suggestions, answer their questions. I have been on the receiving end of this many times. I was fortunate to have been exposed to many decent players like this before I met up with my first egotistical newbie crusher.

Anyway, those are some of my thoughts on what makes a good tournament player. I hope you can use them.

Thanks,
Michael Hawley

Tips to Remember When Playing in a SWCCG Tournament

By John Carr

1. Have a good understanding of all cards that you have included in your deck. This means make sure you know by memory both sides of your objectives and any other cards you are using.


2. Don't be afraid to read an opponent's card. This cost me in my first tournament. I was playing an "Asteroid Sanctuary" deck and completely forgot to draw for Asteroid destiny. My opponent not only caused me to lose three force a turn from AS, but was also able to force drain me with a lone X-wing because I never attempted to knock it out with Asteroid destiny.


3. Any time you don't understand a rule, or a card, ask your Tournament Director to explain.


4. Try to make your deck so that you can compete against some of the common tournament decks in your area. You won't be able to counter every strategy, so don't focus too many cards to shutting other people down, but always be prepared for anything.


5. Decks that are popular today, won't necessary be popular next month. If it has been awhile since your last tournament, try to find out what decks are being played in tournaments. Check the BBS or other SWCCG websites(editor: or Deck Tech!) that post tournament-worthy decks. This will give you some
idea of what you might be up against.


6. Remember, no matter how good of a player you are, you won't be able to win every single game.


7. Be a gracious loser. Congradulate your opponent, and prepare for the next round.


8. Don't whine about how broken a card is, or how insanely powerful a deck is. SWCCG is a game based on Star Wars, but not every game will unfold exactly like the movies.


9. If you didn't win the tournament, ask the winner what decks he played with. This should help you out in future tournaments.


10. Have fun playing! That's what games are for.

John Carr

To Be a Good Player
By Mike Fitzgerald

Dedication-
To be a good player you have to want to be a good player. Your showing that you want to be a good player by simply reading this issue of DeckTech. I consider myself to be a 'good' player. I probably spend at least 10 hours a week searching the Web and various discussion lists looking for new and exciting info and strategies. This number of hours usually doubles and sometimes comes close to tripling when Regionals come around. Take advantage of local gaming nights or , if your like me , try to find a friend and just keep testing new decks against each other with new info you've heard about. What I'm surprised about is that many players don't read Tournament Reports. I think Tournament Reports are where it is at. As long as the writer keeps it interesting and can give a good general description of the decks being played then it is a very valuable metagame asset. The toughest part of dedication is playing when your getting a little bored with the game. I like to create fun decks just for situations to keep me interested. Also , when you've played about 10 games in a row and you keep testing the same decks against each other you get kinda bored quickly too.

Confidence-

You shouldn't walk into any game and think your gonna get killed or just wipe the guy off the table. Try to keep an even flow mentality . If your gonna make a mistake just overestimate the opponent instead of underestimating the opponent. Upsets will happen , thats why we play the game instead of giving the player with the higher ranking the win. I get pumped up when I play high rated and considered elite players because I feel something special is gonna happen ( see a new deck style , cards being used like never before , or the chance of me winning ) . I try to use fun decks at tournaments once in awhile to keep myself from worrying about my rating and testing the current scene. Two weeks before Regionals last year I brought 2 decent Hoth Clouds decks and I got smashed by 34 and 35 so then I decided that those weren't gonna cut it so I had to get into the JP / Anti-JP scene. Also , ratings mean very little. Use it to feel proud of yourself once in awhile but don't go to far. Right after Special Edition I played against a player with about a 1420 rating and I managed to battle it out 48 minutes and get a 1 card win. That was the toughest 2 points of my life , against someone in all aspects I was expected to crush off the table ( never mind that is was a HBase deck ;). The first time that I seriously traveled to a top notch tournament ( besides my local area ) was going down to play v. Gabriel Alonso in Miami. I got to play him with my Darkside ( which I considered my best deck at the time -- side note : don't favor one deck over the other ) . I played one of my most memorable games and managed to beat him by 1. He smashed every character I put out while I wasn't knocking any characters of his out. I managed to shake it off and beat him because I kept my head in the game. I truly believe that confidence will help differentiate between players of equal ability ( if there is such a thing ).

Patience-

I believe this is the greatest asset to a good player. You and your opponent are both gonna make mistakes. The question is who can exploit the mistake to the fullest for the win. I'm gonna use extreme examples of myself since I know myself the best. I don't like babbling over and over about past
Regionals but it helps make my point. In the '97 Dagobah Regionals I was 2-0 and was the 2nd highest player after those 2 games. I played against Brett Helgeson's LS EBO / Droids deck. I was using a Tatooine Drain. I got Vader down to the 4th Marker early and was doing little damage to him ( couldn't find sites ). He was mowing through his deck with How Did We Get Into This Mess , I hadn't seen that before. I got so frustrated because he was just going through his deck at will and I had seen only maybe 20% of the deck and most of it was on the way to the lost pile. Well he had Do or Do not on Table and a grappler on a Sense. Out of feeling helpless I let a Sense fly and cancelled his HDWGITM. He just proceeded to play another. I lost that game by 2 Force. The 2 Force that I lost for wasting that Sense would've saved me the game. Patience. But now well go out of the gloom and doom to the better times. '98 Dagobah Regional. I'm playing v. Rusty Zion's JP Manipulator. He starts with TiNT and has loads of ADT , Voyeurs , and SCrews. He starts right off the bat with manipulation of my interrupt heavy deck. All I do is smirk while he picks works my deck around and tries to get into my head. Somehow I manage to fight him off with the strength of Shocking Info and Grimtaash. I was surprised that I didn't flip out and get impatient because that was a mental exhausting game ( I must've handed my force pile over to ADT about 20 times ) where my patience made the difference.

Hope this helps you out on your quest to become a better player.

Pavlik / Mike Fitzgerald / Just smile and nod , baby. Smile and nod.

 

I think my biggest weakness is…

By Tony Harris

I think my biggest weakness is wearing down in long tournaments. I have in the past played mind intensive decks barawains, off the edge, training, dark side asteriods, etc. decks that are sneaky. My last tournament I played maelstrom local uprising (easy to play) and on the dark, I played a tight ground pounder on dark. Whatever Happened to Vader and Tarkin?
By Paul Maholick

There was a time when nothing created greater fear in the heart of a LS player as when Vader and Tarkin hit the table together. The power 10, ability 9, destiny draw canceling combo was once the best in the game. It forced the LS do generally do one of two things, either 1) concede a force drain of two or three a turn (compliments of Vader’s Saber) or 2) Muster up some mains, toys and/or destiny adders to fight it out, committing resources that might be better spent elsewhere. It seems that things have changed, even though this combo remains competitive, it just doesn’t have the kick it once had nor inspire the fear it once did. Why is this?

The First Shot

I believe that technically Demotion should be considered the first attempt to reign in this combo. Certainly the card had other uses, but its foremost duty was to cancel Tarkin’s game text. However, its susceptibility to Alter proved to be its undoing. It could not be counted on in the heavy Sense/ Alter days of pre-Dagobah, and even after DODN it did not fair that well. Today it is more of a trivia question than a viable strategy, and is most likely destined to never again receive tournament consideration. In fact the only time you will hear the card mentioned these days is in a conversation such as this one.

(Two Players opening packs of Premiere after a tournament)

Player One: What did you pull in your pack? Anything good?

Player Two: No, another Demotion

It All Started to Fall Apart on Cloud City

Cloud City gave us the second blow against the mighty Vader/ Tarkin combo. And a powerful blow it was, as from this point on, it all went downhill for this Imperial duo. Uncontrollable Fury is a card that really penalizes the use of Vader. Besides canceling the destiny canceling mechanism, Vader is in jeopardy of causing the DS player to lose two force each turn if the LS has a character of greater than 3 ability on the table. The DS does get something from the card though, a +2 boost to Vader’s power, but it is hardly compensation for what is lost. True, you can avoid the loss my battling every turn, but once Uncontrollable Fury is played the LS generally takes a hands-off policy toward Vader, preferring to reap the benefits of what amounts to an extra force drain of two each turn . This leaves Elis Helrot as the DS’s only recourse to "get it on." But Elis is not without its own risk, as it can be Sensed, or Quite a Mercenaried. And if you actual initiate battle you have to face the reality that you can’t cancel a destiny draw now anyway. At this point the DS generally throws in the towel, loses Vader, and if lucky deploys an another from hand. If unlucky, Uncontrollable Fury continues to eat away at the DS, taking its toll over the course of the game..

Luckily for the DS, Uncontrollable Fury does have a counter, You Are Beaten. A powerful interrupt in its own right, YAB is the only thing that gets rid of this irksome effect as it is immune to Alter. Unfortunately YAB is not immune to sense, so canceling Uncontrollable Fury is not a sure thing. Truly, Uncontrollable Fury is an evil LS card.

 

Its Worse

When Jabba’s Palace hit the scene, it had no real effect on the Vader/ Tarkin combo. Certainly DS decks were becoming more alien based, but Vader and Tarkin still showed up in Jabba’s Palace, Scum and Villainy be damned. Overall, Jabba’s Palace did little to hurt the combo. But the same can not be said for Special Edition.

SE’s objectives changed the game. Far-reaching global "effects" have come to dominate the tournament environment. Because of the LS’s ability to make your force drains –1 everywhere or cancel drains outright with Hidden Base, the ability to control a single site with the duo becomes less of an advantage. Having Vader and Tarkin at a site no longer equates to steady draining for the DS anymore.

Next, EPP’s make the combo’s ability to cancel a destiny draw less relevant. Get EPP Luke, Han and Leia together and the LS is drawing three destinies already, god forbid the LS is packing destiny adders. So how do you use your canceling ability? Do you cancel a weapon destiny and hope the others miss? Do you wait and cancel the highest destiny draw for attrition that you can find? There is no good answer here. Pass up the weapon draws and most likely Tarkin is going to forfeit for 0, if not Vader as well. Cancel the first weapon hit and the others might still get you. Or if you play it "safe" and cancel an attrition draw the other two might exceed six (Tarkin’s forfeit if it is not 0) and cause both Tarkin and Vader to be lost. Lets face it, with the LS having a chance at so many more destiny draws, the ability to cancel one is nice, but certainly not the game breaker it once was.

If you put all of these together, the situation is bleak. Instead of being a sure damage dealer, Vader and Tarkin are now a target. They can neither drain as effectively given the LS’s potential use of objectives, nor can they battle as effectively as they once could, given their souped-up opposition. To make matters worse Vader remains a target for Uncontrollable Fury, possibly costing you force for even deploying him. No, this is not your father’s Vader/Tarkin combo. This is much worse

 

Help Me HDADTJ, You’re My Only Hope

With all this gloom and doom, I thought it was my duty to leave you with just a bit of hope. HDADTJ, the Vader player’s dream card, breathes a bit of life back into the combo. With the card on the 0 side you can pick Vader up and stick him in your hand. When you do this, all cards deployed on him, including your opponents go back to the owners used pile. So long Uncontrollable Fury. Or if the objective is one the 7 side you can play YAB with worrying about a LS Sense ruining your cancellation.

Furthermore, now that you can start an epic duel during a battle, you can use Tarkin to cancel a duel destiny. It makes dueling much easier. Imagine the situation. You have Vader and Tarkin across from SOS, Han with Blaster and Leia with Blaster. Generally this would be very bad news. You initiate battle and start a duel with Son of Skywalker. You cancel the five he drew, leaving him with three. You win the duel easily and Luke crosses over. Now you have to worry about the battle, with the LS getting two destiny draws (it was three before the battle) and some weapons destinies. Sure, Tarkin is most likely going to get shot. Unfortunate? Yes, but he served his purpose and made the LS lose 5 cards and Luke, certainly a good trade for Tarkin. You have one more surprise though. You play Dark Jedi Presence. That is a 15 point power swing and will make the battle much less pleasant for the LS. Net result is that the LS went from having three destiny draws and three weapons with 12 power, to now having 8 power, two destiny draws and two weapons. They also have already lost 5 force before the battle. With DJP, the DS has one destiny draw, and 24 power. Not a bad deal. And with the objective now flipped to the 7 side, you can sense those nasty destiny adders they were playing to play. Yes, the LS could still get lucky and hit Tarkin and Luke, but with your higher power, you will not lose the battle and Vader will survive to ensure Visage damage as the LS scrambles to draw for more firepower. HDADTJ doesn’t solve all your problems, but it sure can help.

Conclusion

The Vader/Tarkin combo has been weakened with time. Due to the effect of Uncontrollable Fury and the SE trend toward global force drain modification and the direct canceling of drains, having Vader and Tarkin at a site together just doesn’t yield you as much as it once did. In fact it may end up costing you. Once extremely effective, the combo is now just a "nice to do." Outside of the HDADTJ environment take care when playing the combo. You always had a big target on your chest, only now the LS has additional and indeed larger guns. However, in the right deck, with the right support cards, and a bit of luck, the combo can still be deadly.

Strategy on the current scene

By Michael Josem

Is it possible to build a deck that is able to deal with X-Wing swarms, Operatives, and EPP Swarms?

Dark Side ground decks have a serious problem currently, because Obi-Wan + Luke + Chewie + Han + COAS + Jedi Presence (not a difficult combo to achieve with 4 of each in your deck), will give you a total power of 45. Throw in a Draw Their Fire (Signaled for at the start of the game) and a Frozen Assets, and the DS _will_ lose a minimum of 25 force.

Dark Side space has serious problems against Operatives - the -1 drain is an absolute bitch to avoid - The maximum possible drain is 4, excluding odd occurences such as Alderaan. Since I've only just thought of it, is Alderaan viable? How about we build a CPI deck that has a chance against Ops..... But wait! Then you get smashed by EPP Swarms. Oh well, it was a nice idea.

So, how on earth do we deal with these stupid EPP Swarms?

Let's look at what they're dependent upon:
1) A big battle
2) Lots of Rebel mains
3) Frozen Assets, or the DS player not having a Ghhhk
4) Having something to beat down upon.

Hence, how do we stop any one of those things from occuring (we only need to block one)

1) A Big Battle
What ways do the DS have of stopping a big battle? The only possibility that I can think of is 'Projective Telepathy,' which will only work if the LS has little/no force active. It is possible that it might stop the Jedi Presence. However, Frozen Assets/DTF blocks this option.

2) Lots of Rebel Mains
This appears to be the weakpoint of the Maelstrom/EPP Swarm decks - If you can stop the LS from amassing enough cards to do the beatdown. So, what are the options available?
a) Monnok. The used function of this will prevent the LS player from having more than 12 cards in hand. If you time this correctly (after the first character of the beatdown has been deployed), with a bit of luck you should be able to set up your own beatdown, if hopefully you have disrupted enough cards from the opponent's hand. Otherwise, there is nothing to stop the LS player from drawing up again next turn. Monnok also has an advantage in that it costs nothing to play - You can play it even if the first part of the beatdown is the Frozen Assets.
b) Scanning Crew. This is often found in decks with around 10 copies, which is nice, however this isn't quite as strong because: 1) It requires a force to play and 2) Is less versatile against Operatives and HBX and 3) It gets hurt pretty majorly by Shocking Information

3) Frozen Assets, or the DS player not having a Ghhhk This isn't quite so easy to avoid. Firstly, to be guaranteed of having a Ghhhk in your hand, you're looking at stacking 3/4 Ghhks plus Masterful Moves in your deck. In combination with this, you are required to have some defence against Frozen Assets, and if it is interrupt based (such as Alter or Imperial Supply), (and I can't think of anything non-interrupt based either) then you need to play it before the battle begins, and then give the LS player the choice of whether to continue. To stop the beatdowns, you
need to take control of the game, and to make sure that the LS player is reactive to your moves, and this doesn't cut it.

4) Having something to beat down upon.
Obviously, the LS needs something to beat down. If you are able to prevent the LS from deploying to where you have characters, then they could be in for a bit of trouble. Let's look at the possibilities:

  1. Using sites with no LS force icons. Too hard to defend - Any LS player worth his salt will be playing with Nabrun or Spies or preferably both. I do not think that it is feasible to have a copy of Revealed in hand every turn, nor to block multiple Nabruns
  2. Using JP Sites with None Shall Pass. These things are only worthwhile in large numbers, and are frighteningly vulnerable to Grimtaash. You will also require one every turn for the Obi-Wan, and the force advantage this card generates isn't really effective, since all but the most poorly designed Beatdown decks will have ample force generation. Playing in Space/in the Clouds. This seems to be the easiest, and most fool-proof method - If you do not deploy anything to the ground, you can't get smashed. But space decks have a chronic problem against Operatives....

    This game is far too similar to Rock/Paper/Scissors (talking of which, I've discovered that playing R/P/S against yourself is a great way of alleviating boredom)

    Have a good one,
