We knew something had to change, so we went looking for solutions.

What we found was a simple statement that revolutionizes the way we thought of DeckTech.

"The progress of any organization is directly related to the speed at which the whole can process information"

We could see this economic principle happening right before our eyes with DeckTech. Our inability to process the information was hindering our success as an organization. We needed a way to turn around and process information globally instantly, in real-time, with no human weakness.

We needed a new information exchange model something that combines the best of what is available to us right now. So we created the datashare concept. This is an exciting time for us here at DeckTech. If you’re interested in finding out more about the datashare concept and how it effects us check out the next article.

Simplifying the Schools

When you simplify it there are basically 2 schools of deck design in SW: CCG.

The Deck of the Month Club and the Pro Players Club

The DOTMC says pick a few things to do really well, focus, focus, and focus. And like a laser you can penetrate any other deck design. This school of thought often time neglects the opponent altogether and only focuses on accomplishing a specific objective or two. Many times this results in speed decks or lockdown decks. These decks perform fairly well 85% of the time but usually fails at the worst possible time. Like in a big tournament. The reason is they are focused to the point were they have real weakness that hopefully you can't exploit because of their strong offensive moves. In big tournaments there is always one player who can see your weakness and exploit it.

Another negative to the DOTMC is that the life cycle of a typical deck of the month is so short thus the name. This stems from the nature of the lethality of these decks design. According to the paradox of lethality the more devastating the weapon you use against your enemy the faster they counter to defend against it. The ultimate goal of a DOTMC member is to construct a deck that wins every time, without fail regardless of what your opponent is playing. When they achieve even something close to this then Decipher usually steps in and errata's the cards. Most of the time they don't accomplish that but manage to generate fairly lethal decks, which of course get countered.

The skill in being a member of the DOTMC is in building the deck. I know lot's of players who are DOTMC players and they are good players no doubt but they really shine when building their decks. The quick turnaround necessitates that they become better and better and faster and faster. Many can look at a deck list and play the first turns in their mind and tell you all the problems that your going to face and which decks will beat you and which you will win against. They are excellent meta-gamers they themselves control the meta-game. Like the force they bend it to their will. They are too cards focused and not game focused though. Because they play with different decks all the time they never really get a firm grip on any one deck. The elusive strategies that would have reveal themselves had they continued with a deck often times vanish when the new decks are made.

Because of this they usually have problems with the next group of players, the Pro Players Club.

The Pro Players Club is opposed to changing their decks very much at all. Sometimes they spend an hour trying to change out a single card only to change it back just before a tournament. Why? The PPC is not about the decks it's about the game. The deck is built to allow the player to control, combat, and dismantle the other player. The PPC motto is not "build a deck that will win", but rather "build a deck that will give me a chance to win". They play well-balanced decks. They play decks that give you a chance to win against everything. And then they practice, practice, and practice with those decks until they know them better that they know themselves.

They know that Figrin Dan is a good force retrieval card just like the DOTMC players do. But they also know that he is a good way to set up destinies, and look at your opponents deck, and a way to hold off AAA for a turn, and a way to block a drain late in the game and a way to throw your opponents destiny tracking off … and on and on. It's not the deck, it’s their playing that makes a difference. Many have an entire set of rules they personally follow that they have constructed from experience. What to do against this, what to do against that etc. It should come as a surprise to no one that when they go to build or rebuild after an expansion that they try do design a deck that gives them a chance to counter all of the following.

Opponents Offensive Objectives:

Force Drain - cause you to discard by controlling drainable locations

Battle Damage - cause you to discard by causing him battle damage

Energy Choke - prevent the you from doing anything by denying him necessary force

Effect Damage - playing an effect that causes the you to discard

Interrupt Damage - playing an interrupt that causes the you to discard

Manipulation - using several methods to prevent you from using your cards or increasing his card use

Intelligence Gathering - using several methods to discover what you are playing

Psychological Assault - using several methods to cause you mental and emotional disruption

Block Regeneration - prevent you from regenerating any life force

Place out of Play - putting your critical components out of play

Opponents Defensive Objectives:

Block Force Drains - prevent you from force draining them

Block Battle Damage - preventing you from causing them battle damage

Prevent Energy Choke - preventing you from getting a force choke hold on them

Cancel Effect Damage - preventing or canceling any damage caused by effects

Cancel Interrupt Damage - preventing or canceling any damage caused by interrupts

Prevent Manipulation - preventing you from manipulating either of your cards

Counter Intelligence - preventing you from discovering their cards

Counter Psychological Warfare - preventing you from disrupting them mentally

The Pro Players will make decks that can counter all of these objectives. Thus after having done that are not inclined to change their deck much at least until the next expansion. The problem with the PPC is that often times refusing to change often leads to stagnation and antiquation. They become relics and often times get swept away by DOTMC because they refused to admit they old-style decks are not good enough anymore. Still if you are considering becoming a truly great SW: CCG player you will need to master the balanced deck. Just keep yourself updated and current. And play a lot.

Of the two types which would you say you more comfortable with? You will probably take the road most of us do. Start as a DOTMC and move into the PPC and then visit the DOTMC every once and a while just to make the game interesting. What ever you do, do it well and you will succeed.

David M. Akers

Players Guide:

As you see, this deck is a customized version of HDADTJ, but there are some few important changes that give this deck much more freedom.

1st point: no The Circle Is Now Complete, so you have 2 cards slots free, and less "must have" cards in hand.

2nd point: no destiny adders or duel adders, so here again you have other slots free.

3rd point: it has a quick setup, with 8 battleground sites and 6 Vader.

Strong points:
1st point: 2 Trample, 2 SE Vader

2nd point: 5 destiny 7 (but only 4 in your life force) that you can recirculate with Reactor Terminal

3rd point: Trample will work against Ewoks, so you are in good position to face Endor.

4th point: Come Here You Big Coward just in case (starting effect if you face No-Shelter with Revolutions)

5th point: no Visage defense (it would require too many cards to do it effectively) but just another Visage ready for service.

The main tactic is to put Vader down early at a battleground site (1st or 2nd turn) to flip the objective as soon as possible. Then, if there is no opposition, this deck basically spreads out with Walkers to drain for the kill. When Obi shows up, you just have to Choke him via a destiny 7 (or Trample him) and crush the remaining opposition. This deck usually arranges 2 destiny 7

in a row to duel succesfully Luke (no need of destiny adders, then). This pretty much reduce the fear of EPP strikes. Especially since they won't be able to Sense/Alter anything.

Situations:

1) operatives: usually (if they are not starting with Goo Nee Tay) you can deploy Vader first turn, which usually means you've won. This deck choke and Trample everyone, and with also J'Quille, operatives tend to be slaughtered.

2) HB: well, the quick setup allows this deck to deal a substantial amount of damage in the beginning, meanwhile you just Alter their effect and lose your Death Squadron Star Destroyers (to be retrieved later via Put All Sections On Alert to probe when you are ready). Usually you manage to find it. Also spreading to drain at 3 different sites means that your opponent will always suffer damage each turn.

3) EPP Swarm: this (im my opinion) has always been the nemesis of HDADTJ, since LS is way too powerful with the EPPs. Indeed, LS is faster, but against this deck, thanks to the objective, tends to lose. The reason is one simple one: when you did just Trample or Choke Obi-Wan Kenobi, there isn't much your opponent can do anymore to damage you. Obviously, no-one told you that you couldn't Choke and/or Trample someone else.:)

Strategy Notes:

Again, I constructed this deck as *fun* answer to LS M&T. As such, this is a *battle* oriented JP deck, no Gailid, and not for the faint of heart. Against LS M&T, start with Bad Feeling Have I to give you a little more time to get your aliens out. First, look for ETE so you can put it on the Audience Chamber (nothing new). This, coupled with JP's text, will let you find whatever aliens you need to complete your hit squad (Jabba, Boelo, Fett, Evazan). If they send someone to block JP's text (it's usually too early for them to send anybody exceedingly strong), send what you have to confront them, use JP+Chamber text to grab someone else, and move where it suits you best; repeat the process as needed if you can't seem to get them off of JP. After you got your hit squad assembled, get out Miyoom (note: cancel Goo Nee Tay ASAP). She mainly serves as a diversion; first she forces the LS to diligently monitor their hand, second she kills naughty rebels (duh). If your local meta packs heavy counters to her, Dannik Jeriko is a great replacement;

his out of play text is very useful against LS Mains. It's usually best to save the Vader/Tarkin combo until they've made some kind of commitment to the ground or space (of course, the exception to this is if you need VAder to SA stuff); this way they aren't stuck where they're not

needed.

The deck has very concentrated fighting force that doesn't work very well in pieces, so you have to make sure to optimize the battles you fight as they represent the deck's major means of hurting the opponent. When you're ready to battle, check their hand with Monnok (look for Quite a Merc, Hioujx, good battle cards, etc), make an effective (this means setting up destiny to Sense It's a Hit) transport w/Elis, and administer *effective* beatdown (drop First Strike for Hioujx, Sense any nasty interrupts, and use your character removal). Hopefully, it should only take a couple of *effective* battles to seal the game. If they move into space, the Bus, Executor, Vader's Shuttle, and the ORSs can give them a headache, especially if you saved your Vader/Tarkin combo.

Against HB, start with Expand the Empire to up your activation, so you can start looking for your ships. Again, your object is to beat them at their own game. Sense, Alter, Monnok, and There'll Be Hell To Pay effectively shut down their pesky effects and interrupts. From there, you need savvy battling. A loaded Space Cruiser (Jabba, Boelo, Fett, and an ORS) is deadly, giving you total immunity, free movement, two destiny, and one canceled destiny. If they deploy Tatoonie, simply cancel ETE on the Palace, redeploy to the Audience Chamber, and look for the guys you

Need to load up the Cruiser. The Executor, Vader/Tarkin, The Bus, and Personal Shuttle speak for themselves. Also, Miyoom can stow aboard a ship and offer status reports on their hand.

Once you establish control over their interrupts and effects, you shouldn't have much trouble

battling and draining them till they die. Force Drain? What's that? Battle, baby, Battle! Oh yeah, most importantly, Have Fun.

Joseph R Pigg

They expect to see someone start with an Objective with which they can classify the deck right off the bat ( I've only seen very few creative ideas off objectives). Also the force choke aspect is very nasty , you make them save force ( slight choke ) for an interrupt that in all likelyhood you

will just cancel with Sense or lockdown with Frozen Assets. Of course you will want to modify the deck towards your local meta and personal tastes but you should have fun playing it and gaining interaction , which to me is the best part of the deck.

Mike Fitzgerald

Deck Partitioning
partition (n.): something that divides or seperates.

Whenever I tell people that I partition my deck, they usually ask me what I mean. I respond to them with generally the same stuff that I'm going to write in this article. Deck partitioning can be executed many ways. In this article, I'm going to go over the pro's and con's of each way, and tell you which method I myself recommend. After reading this and trying deck partitioning out once or twice, I think that it ultimately will help you in your overall game play.

Before I go into the types of deck partitioning, I want to explain what it is. Basically, it's just saying like "OK, I'm gonna dedicate only 30 cards to getting so and so done. No more, no less. And then I'll dedicate 10 cards to keep Force drains from hurting me much, and so on and so forth".

I. Partition by Card Type

With this partition method, you'd basically just decide on how many characters, locations, etc. you wanted, and keep that constant true throughout the deckmaking process. I can't really see any advantage that you'd allow yourself to receive by doing this, except that you'd always be able to approximate how many cards of that specific type you had remaining in your deck at any given time. But really, you should know that anyway. But I suppose that partitioning this way could make it easier to remember. Try it and tell me!

II. Partition by Destiny Number

This method of partition is, in my opinion, only somewhat helpful. This type of partitioning, obviously, is only really good for decks that rely on high destinies (such as Sabbac or real battletype decks), but not really useful at all unless you say "Alright, I'll dedicate 15 destiny 4 cards that will be used solely for battling". So this really could help you because you'd have your battle deck, but also know which destinies you'd have left, etc.

III. Partition by Ultimate Goal

I'm not sure that's exactly the best way to say this, but I'll explain. This partition method is my personal favorite, and I often use it (especially with the objective decks). Basically, you just say "I wanna dedicate 30 cards to getting Luke's butt trained as fast as possible. I wanna dedicate another 20 to battling/drain sites. And the rest will go to hindering my opponent". That's more or less (mostly less ;) how I partitioned my Mind What You Have Learned deck. This method is wonderful. It really lets you break your deck down, so that you have the bare essentials to pulling off several different strategies, but also a few extras to enhance those strategies. Generally, the only problem with this (which is a problem with all decks, partitioned or not) is that your deck will be too large. If this happens, simply increase the partition on one category, and reduce it on the other. And if that doesn't work, you're just gonna have to scratch some cards.

IV. Partition by All of the Above

This is no doubt, one of the better methods of partitioning. All you have to do is, using the third method as a base, figure out the utopian way that you can splice card type, destiny, AND general strategy into one partition.

So overall, partitioning greatly enhances card efficiency. It's not only a good way of building decks, but taking them apart too (making sure that you don't have any uncescessary cards in there). So try partitioning your decks for the upcoming tournament. If it works for you and you wanna keep doing it, that's cool. If it doesn't work for you and you wanna refrain from doing it, that's cool too. Just be sure to use whatever deckbuilding method that you're most comfortable with. Until next article, see ya later!

In fact, these cards are so deceptive in their purpose that they often fool players into leaving them out of their decks. They lack the obvious, straightforward punch of other cards and are often overlooked - fortunately for some of us.

My Favorite Mistakes

I was eager to write about this particular topic because, quite frankly, it is a topic that I know something about. I have made many, many, mistakes in my tournament experience. Hopefully by sharing them with you, you will be able to avoid them in your gameplay.

Patience is a Virtue

One of my biggest problems for a time was rushing my game. Sometimes I did not give my strategy a chance to set up, especially if my opponent was off to a fast start. Now do not get me wrong, having a deck that is flexible enough to respond to an opponent’s strategy is a good thing. And being able to adapt your own strategy and play style to defeat your opponent is an admirable quality. But it is often a thin line between when you should stick to your guns and when you need to start playing defense.

For example, once I was playing a MWYHL/Mains deck. MWYHL was just a force generation engine. The heart of the deck was designed to win the game in one decisive battle. The general game plan was to get Yoda’s Hut, and Yoda out on the first turn. Then I deployed my mains to Cloud City interior sites (to avoid walkers) along with Pucimir, and then possibly a Boshek or some other expendable ability 4 character to Dagobah using the first Jedi test. With Pucimir and the first Jedi Test complete, the DS was forced to spread out if they were to win a force drain race. Once they spread out I was generally only a Draw Their Fire/Frozen Assets/Clash of Sabers/Jedi Presence/Nabrun away from victory.

So in one game I was playing against an ISB deck. It was not drop and drain, but my opponent seemed to get it working in no time. He dropped the Audience Chamber on his first turn. This was followed next turn by Jabba and an ORS. I noted that the ORS now had a forfeit of five. "Nice.", I thought. More big Tatooine drain sites came out, along with ORS’s and probe droids. The Audience Chamber game text was used to get Ak-Rev, who then pulled Rystall. So he was doing musician retrieval as well. His drains were taking their toll and mine were not. I was having trouble finding my mains. Eventually I got my band together and was ready to go... almost. I had EPP Han, Chewie, EPP Leia, Obi, and Luke, a Jedi Presence, a Don’t Get Cocky, a Clash of Sabers, and a Nabrun. What was missing? Frozen Assets. Draw Their Fire was on the table, but I was missing the second part of the combo.

Well, I was getting drained pretty badly. I figured if I did not do it now, I would lose on drains. So I went for it. I declared Nabrun. He had no response. I paid my transport cost. No Oota - Goota. I was relieved. I initiated battle and won. 4 to 43 or so. I didn’t even use Clash, as I had played Scramble already. I thought I had won, smashed him, nailed him. Then he Ghhk’s.

Ouch! I did not know it right away, but that was the game.

Frozen Assets was one of my destiny draws, so it was not too hard to find again. By the next turn I had drawn another Nabrun and a Frozen Assets. This time I thought I had him. I declared Nabrun. He plays Tarkin’s Orders. Tarkin’s Orders? He didn’t have a ship in his deck. Those Rebels Won’t Escape Us I could believe, but not Tarkin’s Orders. Anyway, I hunted for another Nabrun, but the drains were too much. By the time I drew it I didn’t have enough force in my deck to make sure I could pay for transport and use weapons and draw destiny. I activated 3, as much as I could activate and still have an effective battle. I drew a five for Nabrun’s cost. I was done. He drained me next turn, and that was it.

After the game he told me he had taken his Imperial Supply’s out of his deck. He did not believe that anyone in this area played the DTF/FA combo. So I blew it. If I had waited another turn, I could have won that game. But I moved too soon, abandoned a piece of my game plan and paid the price. Now I am much more willing to give up a drains for a turn or two rather than mount a half-hearted, ineffective offensive. It was a good, if painful, lesson to learn.

Concentrating on the Wrong Thing

This is the problem that is currently causing me the most problems. Believe it or not, I am forgetting to rather important things, like retrieve force. I currently am running an ISB/Mains mix. There are not a ton of agents, so I usually can not retrieve every turn. However, I have lost several games because I forgot to retrieve an agent when I had one to retrieve. How do I miss it? I find on focus on the other parts of the game, and I just lose my head.

For example, I was playing against a Hidden Base deck. It was end game. We were both set up at our draining sites. After much battling, I had rescued Coruscant from the rebel scum, but TPIFF was targeting Tatooine and it was hurting my drains. It would be while before I could get my fleet down to Dagobah. And at this rate they would be more useful blocking the LS drains. So I turned my attention to how to best blocking those drains without getting smashed. It was one of those games that they person who drained last was going to win. I had five cards in my life force, and my opponent drained for five. He won with a differential of two. One more turn and I would have won. If I had retrieved only one more agent, I would have won the game. But I didn’t

And it is not just a DS problem. In a recent Bespin & Beyond tournament, I was playing a Cloud City Celebration/ Devaronian/ Tessek deck. My opponent was playing a DS operative deck. I had just set up the Celebration. I had Pucimir on CC and Tessek in the Audience Chamber. My drains were going to be big. But the turn after I set it up, out came Coruscant and ABCTTU. With the flipped objective and that effect, I had no drains on Cloud City. My concentration went to how to get rid of ABCTTU, and I forgot to retrieve for 3 turns. Even at 2 a turn, that is six force that I missed. It was stupid. And again the person that drained last won. And again it was not me. With six extra force on my side, it would have been a win.

Like Episode One teaches us, we must be mindful of the future, but not at the expense of the moment.

Sweating the Small Stuff

Here is a collection of some of my other notable screw-ups. The stories are not as good, but hopefully they will still prove to be a cautionary example.

1) Even if you have a counter to your opponent’s play, read the card! For example, don’t let your opponent deploy TK-422 unless you have an Imperial at the site. Once I missed that. I had a walker with an alien pilot. Down came TK-422. I didn’t notice the issue until the next turn, right after the two tramples I had in my hand failed to take him out of action.

2) Don’t try to count cards unless you know what you are doing. It was a sealed deck. I had control of Tatooine the system, while my opponent was draining for two at the Tusken Canyon. I had It’s A Hit in my hand. All I had to do was to make sure I drew It’s A Hit every other turn. I counted my life force, 8 cards. I played the interrupt and moved two ships. So I thought, "Hey, I need two force for next turn, so activate 11." I did, I moved two ships again, and I drew one card. No It’s A Hit. I drew again, and again, and again. Nope, it was not there. "What happened?", I wondered. Given that this was sealed deck and I had no Reactor Terminal every card I drew in excess of the amount I was being force drained cost me differential. Not to mention the fact that if I had done this right there would not be a drain at all. But I blew it. Now I still try to rig destinies, but usually only in friendly games, or if it is the top card using some type of interrupt. The moral here is experiment at the game shop and not the tournament table.

3) Whatever deck you decide to play, make sure you play test it extensively first. I have learned that it is the well-tested and tuned deck that wins, not the new "hot" deck. Many people search the internet for deck ideas. This is a good idea. But hunting the internet for actual decks is not. Maybe this works for some. But I have never had much success using anyone else’s deck. Sure, those decks beat the lesser skilled opponents that I face, but the good ones always seem to shut me down. The decks I win with are the ones I have played with the longest. They have gone through the most iterations and have received the most testing. If you see someone has a good tactic or combo in his or her deck, by all means learn from it. But like so many of my grade school teachers told me, "Parroting is not learning." Remember that internet decks have been built in a different region. And regions vary in the cards that are popular. Some regions like numbers, some never play them. Some like Sense/Alter, some like Grimnok, others don't play with any of those cards. Increasingly this is a game of match-ups, and tuning your deck to the environment is what separates the winners and the losers. Know your local metagame and build around it. Don’t believe that downloading a deck will save you the trouble of doing the work required to win.

Summary

In summary, try to learn from your mistakes, but also those of others. The more mistakes you see, the less likely you are to make them yourself. The fewer lessons you have to learn the hard way the better.

Abstractions from Magic to SWCCG

Or how Brian Weissman helped create Maelstrom!?!


Decipher refers to it as "the OTHER game". Swccg players often call it "the game with all that errata". I often refer to it as "the number one card game for a reason".

Swccg players are foolishly snubbing Magic. As the first card game, a wealth of very serious strategy has built up in Magic, to completely understate the matter. While it is true that the two games differ to some extent, many basics apply to both games and can easily be transferred over. Nearly all competitive events are similar psychologically. Don’t crack, don’t "tip your hand ", remain calm, don’t underestimate your opponent. Take basic card playing strategy, such as don’t burn out your hand needlessly, examine the other players cards out, try to anticipate what he may deploy next turn based on his resources or what he has already played. Also, know the local area and the gamers, be on guard for cheating. While these comparisons are quite obvious with a little thought, an area that is not as obvious in similarity is in deck construction.

Remember that Magic is much older than swccg and the players are just as serious about the game, probably more due to the pro-tour. Very, very smart people have poured hours and hours into constructing decks that, in their time, could crush nearly all opposition. Some ideas were better than others and managed to hit underlying themes, hidden "patterns", if you will, and have outlasted their rivals, and spawned numerous decks based on the same principle. These are usually referred to as an Archetype. Now, my idea has been that perhaps the same "hidden patterns" exist unbeknownst to us in swccg. With that being said, let’s break down a few Magic deck types, and you’ll see where this ends up, and strangely it ends up at maelstrom, which, unerrated was the most powerful deck I had ever seen.

Ok, back in the days when Magic first started the first actual archetype, one which, of course is still used today, was landkill. By destroying the opponent’s power resources, you made it impossible for him to generate enough energy to stop you. This is a basic warfare technique, cut off the enemies’ supply lines. In Magic, this is frequently done by destroying an opponent’s land, or by making it unusable, through cards such as Winter Orb. This is now basically known as "The Prison" in Magic. Swccg has also come across this, and although destroying land is very difficult, only a few CPI decks really try this, locking down the opponent’s force generation is very strong. This explains the strength of decks like Pavlik’s No Shelter. By Revolution-ing the opponent’s force generation early you are able to build up a nearly unstoppable force. Cards such as Beggar and Frozen Assets also work well with this. Their also was once a Sleen, Imperial Domination, Shot in the Dark deck, where you’d quickly remove all the opponents force generation, and use Broken Concentration to remove his only force. This basically caused the errata of Shot in the Dark. Revolution, Beggar, Broken Concentration, as well as others, these cards will always be powerful, and will always have to be guarded against, because they are not gimmicks, they break through to the core of what is happening in the mechanics of the game.

Next deck type nearly directly transferable, " The Permission Deck". In a permission deck, your offense is nearly minimal, but the idea is to thwart everything the opponent tries to set up, and when he’s run out of options, you either ping him to death, or crush him very quickly with a large unstoppable attack. The heart of these decks, in Magic are generally removal spells, and more importantly, Counterspells(and their variants) and Disenchants. These cards run almost parallel with Sense and Alter. Now Decipher has gone a long way in trying to ruin sense and alter for some unknown reason (I guess they don’t think they are "fun" or something), but even so the ability to stop someone’s plan of attack at a crucial moment will ALWAYS be very powerful. Turn back the clock 2 ½ years, before Cloud City came out. What was the Light side playing? More often than not, Dagobah Revolution. Sit on Dagobah where it’s safe, get great warrior going, get some spies down and Sense and Alter EVERYTHING! When ready, throw down the revolutions and sit back and watch the drains come. A Permission deck by any other name, and a powerful one at that. Dagobah clouds, same thing before Special Edition, after setting up the defense, get a few wusses in the clouds for a couple drains a turn, even with Imperial Decree, stop everything via sense and alter, and drain preventers, and let the pinging begin! Once again, very powerful.

Let’s go from the sublime to the ridiculous, and talk about speed decks. First turn Juzams and Juggernauts, meet Turbo Dark Deal. By setting up a massive offense very quickly, kill the opponent before he has a chance to even set up. Force Drains of 16 on the third turn in the unerrated operatives, to people Hurling Ball Lightnings before they die for 12 Hit Points, it can be just sickening. Due to the many ridiculous decks created in this style, from both games, I’m not even going to discuss it, just mention it. I’m sure any of us could think of all manners of crazy speed decks.

I’ve got two things from Magic left to discuss, and I’m going to take a break and talk about one that I doubt is transferable, but, by putting it out in the open who knows, maybe someone will see something I haven’t. I have seen an Inkling of this in only one deck I’ve made and I’ll share it and see what you guys can cook up. This is the Archetype known as "The Reanimator" deck. This is primarily the realm of Black in Magic, by using discard cards and effects such as Merfolk Trader and Bazaar of Baghdad, you actually TRY to throw stuff into your graveyard. Ok, sounds stupid, but there are a few strange cards in Magic, namely Living Death, Animate Dead, Shallow Grave and Buried Alive, and a couple creatures I won’t go into here. These cards let you get stuff OUT of your graveyard, and cheaply! You try to throw out some incredibly HUGE, unstoppable creature, say a sliver queen as quickly as possible, that would normally be almost impossible to get out, then animate him(I guess her in this case), and all of a sudden your opponent is looking at some 7/7 creature on the third turn! It’d be like if on the first turn of the game, you threw out the executor, then on the second, put him down via an effect on the opponent’s lone x-wing and battled! Get the idea? The really strange thing about this, is if you can get the cards back, it becomes a great way of manipulating your Library (reserve deck) because you now know exactly where the cards you want are! Ok, (secret tech time) so the card that comes closest to this is Tibrin. I used to see so many people laugh when I put this out in my space decks that, if laughing caused a loss, I’d be winless. Fortunately, the laughers were wrong. Throwing three force out is not bad if you get back a card that get you MORE than three force, or prevents the loss of more than three force. Out a card goes, you now know where it is, and back it comes. I used to use it to get Kessel Runs, On the Edges, Obi-wan, spies, ultimatum, you name it!! A truly underrated card. Ok, last abstraction, this is where it gets serious, and this is what the title refers to. The principle we will discuss here is CARD ADVANTAGE. Card advantage is the attempt to outdraw your opponent, to increase the amount of cards in your hand, and decrease the amount in his to an ideal of zero. In Magic, this has led to easily the two most powerful decks of all time, "The Necro deck", and the one that is known simply as "The Deck" (created by Brian Weissman), due to it’s incredible synergy. The Deck is a permission deck that uses every color, and nearly every power card. Once it’s set up allows you to draw 2, 3 or even 4 cards a turn, and the opponent is reduced to zero, and when he draws a card, he either plays it or loses it. The keys to this are counterspells and removal and Moat, knocking out everything, get out a Disrupting Scepter to knock out his cards, and use Library Of Alexandria, Jayemdae Tome, and Ancestral Recall and Braingeyser to draw multiple. If you ever play this, you will be amazed at what it can do, the opponent literally can not do anything, and you are just zipping through your library, until you come to the few offensive cards, and you let him have it, he’ll be done it a few turns.

I started telling our good friend and editor David Akers about The Deck, and going on and on about how playing it on the Net was just an incredible experience, because the opponent could do NOTHING. I was focusing on knocking cards out of the opponents hand, cycling though with How Did We Get Into This Mess (I think I may have it solved, but we’ll see how it holds up). So I remember the fateful day, David says on the phone, "well, I think I’ve got the problem solved the other way, as for drawing cards". We had talked about Droid Merchants, previously, but I was really looking in to reducing the opponent’s hand. By Spaceport Speedering Droids, an incredible amount of force could be generated, and by pulling out a hydroponics station with the farm’s game text, you could continually draw cards, then How did we get into this mess, repeat until you got what you wanted. Perfect I said, let’s try it out. I met him for lunch and we tried it out. The cycle starts, he’s cruising through his deck, and try all the mains down on the second turn against some poor slobs, and bang battle damage galore. I tried it, same result. Later Added EPP mains, Jedi Presence, we were seeing visions of second turn kills, absolutely incredible. Then we said hey put in a landed Falcon, hell, you could get force back as well. David sat on this deck for a long time, I wanted him to sit on it until I went to the Star Wars celebration, but his love of the game (and desire to irritate Decipher) finally won out. This truly shows how card advantage can bring about something incredible, because usually in these card games, you have to WAIT to get the cards into your hand! This ends that (for any that has ever seen a necro deck back in it’s prime you’ll know what I mean). Think of it, always having the card you need, all the time, that’s what I was striving for, and David basically achieved it!

So there you go, Magic is an incredible resource (as well as fun), I think that should be proof enough. Study it, you are wise, ignore it, you are foolish. Who knows what else is out there? I hope this got you thinking. I know no one ever responds when I ask, but this time, let me know via deck tech what you think up and what you think of this article. I have found this to be an Eye-opening experience, and I’m sure nothing but power can come out of this. Also, if people express interest (and maybe even if they don’t!) I’ll write an in-depth analysis of "The Deck" for next time. Go out and play some Magic!

GAME BY GAME ANALYSIS

Round 1, Game 1: LS vs DS (Patrick Leenders):

An easy start at a tournament is always good. I have the feeling I’ve played Patrick some time before, but wasn’t sure. Patrick is a nice guy, who stays cool during the whole game, without making annoying remarks and sees SWCCG as a game as it should be. Not a matter of life and death.

It was a surprise to me to see someone play with the Carbon Chamber Testing-objective. I don’t find this to be a strong type of deck. Patrick chose There Is No Try as his SE. It was a quick game with not many interactions. He played an early Monnok, which caused 3 X-Wings to go to my Lost Pile, which was the biggest damage he caused me. Afterwards he told me was searching all the time for Secret Plans, but I don’t have All Wings Report In so that wouldn’t do much good.

He deployed some characters to drain on CC, but I deployed an undercover spy. Later on he deployed some more characters, but I battled them of the table, releasing Jeroen Webb, which I gave him at the start of the game to freeze. I already had Draw Their Fire on table, so the damage extended. I flipped, drained and won. Result Full Win 2 (+25)

Score: 2 (+25)

Round 1, Game 2: DS vs LS (Ronald Rullens):

Ronal Rullens. I remember playing him somewhere in 1998 and he played Ralltiir Operations at that time. I remember that game especially, which I eventually won, because it was very clear to me he had annoyed me through the whole game that time, questioning my actions. But I give everyone a second chance...

Ronald started out with Tatooine: Obi-Wan’s Party House with no SE. I gave it an 80% possibility it was a No Shelter-deck. I started quickly with drains on three different systems and a lone TIE was defending Wakeelmui, protecting the SFS-effect from being canceled by an Alter. He then deploys the Millenium Falcon, with Han Solo with Heavy Blaster Rifle and Rayc Ryerd piloting it, at a system where I had only one TIE-Fighter. He battled and caused me 24-force damage! My oh my! You’re so brave to fight a tiny TIE with a ship as big as the Falcon!

Thank God I had some backup in my hand, so next turn I deployed the TIEs I had in my hand a couple more using the LOST version of APTW. To amass the loss for him, I played Lateral Damage on the Falcon, making it power and forfeit = 0. I battled him and when we started counting our power, the long awaited whining started... He stated that LD only targets the starfighters power, so it was MF (0)+ HS (3)+ RR (2)+ 2 destiny draws (1 and 4)= 10. But I picked up LD, read and explained it’s game-text to him, but he didn’t believe me as always (I talked to several other players at the end of the tourney, and most of them said the same). He continued sustaining his opinion and I countered it with my version. We called the TD over and Jan told us that my theory was the correct one, so Ronald was only power 5. I caused him a force loss of 15. From then on it was just a drain and retrieve game for me. He Revolutionized Obi’s Hut, but it was too late... At the end of the game he activated all the force he had left and drew it into his hand, thus leaving many cards in my hand which I could recycle to my Used Pile, using Reactor Terminal; a very unsportsmanlike action. Result Full Win 2 (+15)

Score: 4 (+40)

Round 2, Game 3: LS vs DS (Sander Dijst):

Sander hadn’t attended a tourney since the SWCCG World Championships in the USA at the end of 1998. He is good. I hadn’t played him during last weeks, so I wasn’t sure what he was going to do. Sander started out with Hunt down and destroy the Jedi and First Strike as his SE. Sander had something very interesting against the lightside, as he was only playing with Alters and no Senses; a very good strategy because of all those lightside-decks that strive on certain Effects. This makes his opponents also question the use of Grimtaash, because you think he will Sense it. Sander started Monnoking my hand pretty quickly, making me lose key-cards, like X-Wings and some Interrupts. He managed to play Monnok 3 times. Jeroen Webb made it early in the game to the Executor: Holotheatre, canceling VOTE. I drained him the whole game on Kessel. He had some trouble finding my hidden base, but his third probing attempt was correct. We had some battles, but mostly he was in control of the game because the Monnoks were so effectively played at the beginning of the game. Result Full Loss 0 (-15)

Score: 4 (+25)
