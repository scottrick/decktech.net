Preparing for the regional qualifiers Team DeckTech Style.

Wouldn’t you like to have a better chance at winning the World Championship this year? If the answer is yes then you should consider DeckTech Tournament Training Teams. We've got a fast track way to prepare you to be the best you can be for your upcoming qualifier but it’s not going to be easy. You will have to join one of the Tournament Teams and communicate with people from all over the world to hone your skills. Before I get ahead of myself lets take a step back at look at why so many people do poorly at qualifier events.

Peak Performance Phenomenon

You’re the best in your area in fact you’re so good you don’t even have much competition anymore. So you go to a qualifier and expect to do well but for some reason you do lousy. Why?

I think this is because players that come from a small tournament scene tend to reach a plateau in their performance after they reach the top of the pack. While other players in more competitive areas find it tougher to win and try harder to get better and better, thus though they might not win as many tournaments as the former player their capacity to grow as a SW: CCG player is not hindered through lack of competition.

There are also the factors of your local meta not adequately preparing you for a event as broad as the qualifiers. If you live in a very competitive area then you are fortunate not to have to worry as much about these issues. But for those of you that live in low competition areas you are going to have to work extra hard to prepare for your regional events.

Here are some Items you will need to know in order to prepare for your regional.

1. What are the best decks on either side and how do you counter them.

2. What decks can you expect to see a lot of

3. Which Decks will you play and how will you play them to counter the above decks

4. What are common mistakes you can make at the regional events? How do you prevent them?

 

To answer these questions the staff of Team DeckTech has decided to set up Tournament Teams. ‘Team DeckTech I’ will consist of the first 2 people from each region that submit their information and agree to the Team DeckTech guidelines. These players will network together to find the answers to the above questions. By networking with people outside of your region you can communicate openly with people about your play testing, tournament, and other experiences and help each other to overcome the Peak Performance Phenomenon without worrying about facing those same people at a qualifier. The reason that there are two from each region is simple, to increase the probability that members from Team DeckTech qualify and provide you an opportunity to get a friend to join a team with you so that you can practice with them in relative privacy.

Each team will have a Team Captain that person will enforce the Tournament Team Guidelines. If you fail to keep the Team Guidelines you will be removed from the team and your spot will open up to be filled by someone else in your region.

This might sound harsh but this system is designed to help people win. This is for serious players those players that are serious should not be penalized by someone who is not.

 

Here are the Tournament Team Guidelines.

1. Information, strategy, deck ideas, theories and other on topic SW: CCG related information that you receive from one of the other Team Members shall not be disclosed to anyone not in your specific team unless given specific permission from the author(s).

2. There are no Tournament Team Lurkers. You will participate by communicating, designing, play testing, and reporting your findings to the entire team regularly.

Note from Editor. The card reviews will now be based on a 5 star rating loosely adapted from the Inquest star rating. I like it because it can be defined so well.

***** Five stars - These are the cards you see in tournaments time and time again. They are the best at what they need to do. They can fit in nearly any deck, and generally have excellent stand-alone value. ex. Darth Vader

**** Four stars - These are also very good cards, but often are a notch below the quality of a five star card, or lose a lot of effectiveness without supporting cards. Often seen in tournaments, but generally in specific deck types. ex. How did we get into this mess?

*** Three stars - These are average cards. They may be important in very specific deck types, but generally have few outstanding capabilites and are not often seen in constructed decks, or are used mostly when an expansion first comes out. Often can be useful in a sealed deck. ex. astromech droids.

** Two stars - A poor quality card. They are either too slow, too expensive, or have some kind of prohibitive drawback that makes them not very useful. Often there are other cards that fulfill similar functions, but much better. Almost never seen in a constructed deck, but in a sealed deck they might make it. ex. Nikto

* One star - A useless card. Often serves no particular use, or is very easily disrupted. Never seen at tournaments. ex. Ghoel

Introduction:

The Executor these days seems like a relic from the past; when the empire's high powered vehicles and starships meant something. But nowadays for the price of a blizzard walker, the rebels can deploy a power of 30, and for a star destroyer, a power of 24 with immunity to attrition! Still power 12, immune to attrition < 12 is a unique card. So where does it all stand? Well, that's what were going to try to find out.

Text:

Executor - Imperial Starship. Capital: Super-Class Star Destroyer

Destiny Draw 1

Deploy 15, Forfeit 15 - Power 12, Armor 12, Hyperspeed 2

3 Permanent Pilots, Astromech, scomp link

May add unlimited pilots, passengers, vehicles, and starfighters. Has ship docking capability. Permanent Pilots provide total ability of 3. Immune to attrition<12.

Summary.

So now we have the Executor in a world that has changed. Let's break it down by starting off with the good. First, a number of people commented on the unlimited pilot ability. Strangely, I have found this to be useless. I don't think I've ever not had extra pilot room when a Star Destroyer was out. So let's move on. With a power of 12 there is no doubt that this ship has some serious mass, but in the current seen mass by itself doesn't hold up, power deploy ratios seem much more important. Take the x-wing, which everybody mentioned. Essentially it boils down to this, for a deploy of 2, you'll get a power of at minumum 3, maximum around 6(s-foils, organized attack). For 8 force, you'll get a power of 20-24 compared to 15 force for a power of twelve. Granted 4 cards to 1, but when playing a l.s. space deck, you are more than likely to see the above situation. And when you are looking at powers like that immunity to attrition doesn't really mean anything. Also, even immunity to attrition less than 12 doesn't mean much anymore, with EPP mains on the Falcon+Destiny Adders, another Unit commonly seen in Mains and Toys. Immunity would best be used when all of your starships have some kind of immunity, as in Kuat Drive Yards, or All Power To Weapons. Forfeit 15, now that's nice.

Now for the bad, as if the good wasn't bad enough. Hyperspeed of 2. This is just low enough to actually cause trouble. You can easily find yourself stranded, especially if you are just "throwing the executor in there" to fight in space. Destiny of 1, bad, but par for the course. Finally the thing I have always disliked, deploy of 15 force. I hate to play with cards that as a magic friend of mine put it, "You die holding". There are plenty of times when the Executor is undeployable. And an undeployable card is useless.

With all that being said, the Executor at times, or in the right decks, can be useful. It's not completely fair to penalize it because the light side cards are so much stronger, as a dark side player you have to compare it to its dark side counterparts, and they are not looking that much better right now. To probe for hidden base, TIE's and ghhks would probably be better, and tallon roll looks strong despite the errata. I think as Mike Fitzgerald put it, it's best use might be as a "home run swing"(excellent phrase). Try to monnok out any barriers, grimtaashs, etc, and throw down on a lone x-wing. It's just enough to keep the opponent from getting sloppy, and if he does you could really punish him. But it's going to cost A LOT of force!

Conclusion: The Executor is too slow, and too easily outclassed by opponents space fleet. It's drawbacks are ugly, and it's benefits are are not really that powerful.

 Review by Usman

The Executor. That word strikes fear in the hearts in many a light side SW:CCG player. Should it? Is it all that great? Should it rank among the Dark Side elite, joining such cards as Darth Vader, Boba Fett, Dr. Evazan, etc.? Well read ahead and find out for yourself. Decipher has certainly attempted to make the Executor something of a God among DS starships. It has unlimited capacity for on-board characters, vehicles, and TIEs. It has a supreme attrition immunity of >14. It has its own unique sites for cryin' out loud! Six of 'em! There is no doubt that it is the best DS starship. You could even make an Executor deck! All you need is Flagship ops, Flagship, Fondor, Visage, a few ties, a few imps, and some gimmick objective (ISB Ops or HDADTJ) and you are good to go! Good to go, if you are smart. Don't kid yourself, the Executor is not invincible, far from it! 12 power is actually not that much! 3 souped up X-Wings could beat the hell out of it. The key to playing the Executor wisely is to not rely on it with all of your deck. It is not that hard to take it down so be on the guard. An easy way to get around this is to play with many high-forfiet low-deploy characters and/or TIEs. Keep your Executor at a location that you can quickly evacuate in case the rebels come at you with all they've got. The immunity to attrition means that even if it is alone at a site, it is nigh impossible to get rid of it without some Jedi Test 5-Multiple destiny adder combo (in other words, it is impossible to get rid of).

The Executor is truely a force to be reckoned with. It is one of the few starships that can be the center of a deck. As with any center of a deck, it must be protected. For example, what good would an EBO deck be without ground defense? Similarly Hidden Base without space defense? With a little defense, the Executor is a menace to light side decks as a whole. Based on this, I give the Executor a 10 out of 10 rating.

In this article I attempt to quantify one aspect of destiny manipulation with The Dark Path, namely its ability to maximize a destiny draw at the expense of two force. I choose to apply the math to this context because the best use of this card has been in the HDADTJ decks that attempt to epic duel. The main thrust of my inquiry is whether or not this effect is worth including in a dueling deck. I will solve this problem by estimating its effectiveness at increasing destiny.

Let us begin the modeling process by making the simplification that drawing a card for destiny has NO effect on the probability distribution of the remaining destinies in the reserve deck. This is the only real assumption in the model, and were it not made then there would be no degeneracies and the solution would lose much of its elegance.

Begin by considering the case where only three destiny "classes" are availabe, class A (0-2), class B (3-4), and class C (5-7). We then expound on this model by increasing the number of destiny classes. In this case define the vector X corresponding to the discrete probability densities of these three destiny classes. Let the elements in X be denoted by x[i], where i is a subscript corresponding to 1 for class A, 2 for class B, and 3 for class C. X is normalized to 1.

Understanding of the maximization mechanic is best achieved through use of a tree diagram which will then collapse to a new probability density function.

Defining the Need for Abstraction

By David M. Akers

I have read some articles recently that point to the complexity of the game and infinite possibilities of deck variations even within a definable deck type and attribute this as a primary obstacle in defining meaningful deck types and strategies.

Though all of these arguments have merit what is missing in the primary study of SW: CCG strategy is an appropriate level of abstraction. Therefore when new expansions come out players scramble to find and exploit the new strategies released with that expansion and give up on their current strategies. One of the primary reasons for this phenomenon is that many players find it difficult to separate their cards from their strategy. As a result we have a inadequate system of deck typing based on card constitution (M&T, Numbers.. etc).

Any deck typing system that will be maintainable must be able to differentiate between what a deck consist of (card wise) and what a deck can accomplish (strategy wise). Now I will assume that many of you realize why deck typing and statistical analysis will improve your deck construction methods and will only focus on the abstraction of SWCCG strategy.

Laying the foundation:

As simply as I can put it, both players start with 60 cards in their life force the one who runs out first looses. To some this might seem an oversimplification, I see it as an elegant foundation. Now lets suppose player A deploys their location draws their 8 cards and sits for the rest of the game what would player B have to do to win? In order for player B to win he has to cause the other person to loose 59 cards 8 from hand and 51 from the life force (assuming player A throws from hand first). This of course does not normally happen but it goes too show that in order to win player B must damage player A life force. Almost all strategies inside SWCCG can be divided into offensive (attempting to cause damage to your opponents life force) and defensive (attempting to prevent or repair damage to your own).

A deck can be given an aggressive ratio based on the proportion of cards dedicated to both defense and offense. A rating of 100/0 would be an all offensive deck (theoretically impossible), 50/50 would be equal of both , and 0/100 would be pure defensive deck (theoretically impossible).

Some more astute players will point out and rightly so that one card might be used equally well for defense or offense. For example Myo adds to your battle damage power 3 and he can regenerate when forfeited from a battle. Most cards can be used either offensively or defensively. So each card can have its own aggressive ratio.

To make matters even more complex is the interaction cards have with each other. Vader Light saber harmless by itself is very aggressive when handed to Vader. Any rating system must also incorporate a method for handling synergistic values greater than the the sum of the component part.

Do show how the inner level of strategy used by a deck the rating system needs to differentiate between the different types of damage. Example:

Force Drain: (Bomar, POF)

Battle Damage: (Vader, Tarkin)

Inserts: (AFA, NTMTO)

Other Effects: (Uncontrollable Furry, Asteroid Sanctuary)

Direct Interrupt Damage (Fear, Limited Resources)

And differentiate between the different types of defense:

Force Drain Reducers: (Spies, Imperial Decree)

Battle Damage Reducers: (Bacta Tank, Mantillian Savrip)

Insert Blockers (Resistance, Torture)

Other Effects: ( Death Star Plans, Kessel Run, TBHTP, Grappling Hook)

Direct Interupt Defense (Were Doomed, On the Edge, Abyssian Ornament, YAB, Sense/Alter/Control)

This is by no means an exhaustive list of the various categories but is offered only to demonstrate what an appropriate typing system would need to take into consideration.

So this is a typical deck profiled using this rough deck typing system

Aggressive ratio 59/41

Offense:

FD: ***************
BD: **************************
IN:
OE:
DID:

Defense:
FDB:**
BDR:**
IB:*
OE:***
DID:*********

Obviously this is an inadequate method to demonstrate the deck type but it does demonstrate the following point: Deck types research built on strategy will be maintainable even after new expansions come along.

If I discovered that the above deck type work well I could make several decks with a similar profile using different cards and test and see if the strategy behind the cards is sound not just the cards themselves. Then I could find the cards that are the most powerfully fit this profile. Test it against other deck profiles find which one seems most powerful and refine that deck to a razor edge then when a new expansion comes along I would only need to quantify what cards if any enhanced or damaged my power profile.

The need for this type of abstract deck typing is obvious if this game is to evolve to a higher level. Will some one step forward with a initiative? A initiative to sponsor a deck typing system that is complete and intelligent and takes all the above factors into consideration? Well thats just what I intend to do. In future articles you can expect to see my attempt at laying a solid strategical foundation for clasifying decks. The components are all in place. Look for more to come.

Murdering Today's Decks with Yesterday's Strategies
By: Chris "Grand Nagus" Hunter

Today's tourney scene is rought with ISB decks full of Outer Rim Scouts, Hidden Base decks full of X-Wings, Malestrom decks full of speed 'whoop ass', and Hunt Down/Sienar Fleet decks full of TIEs. It seems that it is impossible to create a deck to effectively deal with these and other winning tourney strategies on a regular and consistent basis; but the truth is that people [in my area at least] have become increasingly daring in the attempt to completely forget about Sense, Alter, and

Monnok/Grimtassh. People have been stocking fewer and fewer cards to deal with these old school 'control elements' with the belief that the threat of anti-control cards are enough to discourage their opponent from building them into their deck design: their wrong.

In this time of diminished preparedness for the SAC-Monnok/Grimtassh onslaught it is time to capitalize on the weakness your foes have unvailed. How do I do that you ask?? Lets look at a few strategies and how 'old school control elements' might be able to help you "murder" your opponent's

deck:

Yarna d'al' Gargan/Well Gaurded

The motive: This card is truly helpful to the swarm decks (i.e. decks which rely on one character/starship/vehicle in multiple) which plague the tourney scene as of late. All of the decks I mentioned in the beginning of the article rely on mass copies of certain cards (e.g. X-Wings &amp; Outer Rim Scouts) to affect good game play and are therefore vulnerable to Monnok/Grimtassh. Most people then crutch these types of decks on Yarna and Well Guarded to keep their doubles safe and usually only include one copy of the card in the deck (sometimes not even starting with it at that!!!)

The opportunity: Well folks, handling these types of swarm decks can be as simple as Altering Yarna/Well Guarded before the opponent can occupy two battlegrounds and then Monnok/Grimtasshing them to kingdom come !!!

:-) And what's more, because so many of today's top decks rely on a similar swarm mentality, this is a strategy usable against any number of decks. The means: On the light side, start with Obi's Hut or use a strategy based around high ability characters (such as Our Most Desperate Hour/Hidden Base) and deploy them as quicly as possible thus giving you the ability to Alter Well Gaurded before your opponent can occupy the two battlegrounds to make it immune to Alter. On the dark side, try Hunt Down and Destroy the Jedi or any deck archetype which promotes getting a high ability character (preferably Vader) onto the table quickly and Alter Yarna before your opponent can occupy the two battlegrounds. Both of these will leave most opponents W-I-D-E open to Monnok/Grimtassh for the rest of the game or at least hesitant to draw cards for a good while as long as you save 4 force.

Retrieval Cards (e.g. Harvest, All Wings Report In/Short Range Fighters, &amp; Kessel Run)

The motive: Many of today's hot decks also rely on some type of continuous or large one-time retrieval of cards. Cards such as Harvest, All Wings Report In/Short Range Fighters, Abyssin Ornament/Nar Shadda Wind Chimes, Kessel Run, and Sienar Fleet Systems allow the opponent to

keep an edge while you often can do nothing about it due to the sheer lack of "silver bullets" [cards which directly counter another card: Quite a Mercenary for example] and/or combat oriented means of dealing with these strategies.

The opportunity: Many of these retrieval cards (if not all) are at least in some small way vulnerable to Sense or Alter. Canceling these cards outright can be devastating to many deck archetypes which depend on retrieval. Not to mention that the look on your opponent's face when you cancel the card she was depending on to keep her deck going is priceless. ;-) Retrieval is a key element to maintaining today's card advantage and beating down any strategy which gets cards back for the

opponent is going to mean a greater chance for victory in the end.

The means: On the light side, once again high ability characters is key, although speed is not always crucial when trying to cancel retrieval. Against Sienar Fleet Systems, try the same as above to deploy fast and Alter Sienar before they get a chance to occupy Wakeelumi and make it immune to Alter. You might also need a strong space presence to beat down ships at Wakeelumi in case you don't Alter before they occupy. Against other types of retrieval such as Short Range Fighters, Abyssin Ornament, and CC Sabaac/CC Casino speed isn't usually key. Just concentrate on getting a character on the table (preferably Obi) that you know will be successful. On the dark side, once again play high ability character friendly strategies. Hunt Down and Destroy is great for this as well as some other strategies which include charachters of ability 3 or more and an average destiny of 2 or less. Against these types of retrieval speed for the dark side is usually not of the essence either. Just deploy and control the environment when your sure you'll draw good destiny. Add Secret Plans to your S&amp;A strategy and LS retrieval is easily managable.

Power Enhancements

The motive: Another key element to today's top decks are power enhancement cards. Cards like Manuevering Flaps, S-Foils, All Power to Weapons/Organized Attack, and Rebel Ambush/Trooper Assault are used to give one the power advantage in any situation. The cards are often the decision maker in battles can make the difference between battling effectively and getting the beat down. On the bright side of things though, all of these cards are immune in one way or another to Sense or Alter!!

The opportunity: These cards are depended upon for most decks to operate effectively. On their own, TIEs, and stormtroopers suck but many people build decks based around them. Moreover, X-Wings and Speeders are decent cards in and of themselves but are often no match for the dark side on their own. Decks built around these strategies are at a serious disadvantage without these power enhancements and taking them away from your opponent is prime.

The means: On the light side, same as above. High ability, good draws and solid use of available resources is good. In this case speed isn't an important element. Most of the dark sides strategies in this area have no way to protect there enhancers without help from other cards

(e.g. There is No Try), so feel free to take your time. On the dark side, same as before with the exception or Manuevering Flaps and S-Foils. These cards can hurt real bad so take the time to work in a speed contingency for Altering these cards before they get out one of the characters necessary to protect their effects or simply build in provisions for taking out these characters at inopportune times for your opponent (e.g. Sniper or Overwhelmed) so your opponent has no chance to respond before your can lay your Alter down succesfully.

The Bottom Line

Sense and Alter are still a viable and valuable part of Star Wars. They are even more so recently because most people have been lulled into a false sense [no pun intended ;-) ]of security due to either the sheer threat of counters such as Do or Do Not/There is No Try or the belief that they can successfully (and in short order) qualify cards, like S-Foils and Well Guarded, for immunity to 'control elements' successfully. Destroying their crutch cards before they can protect themselves or even taking away that one card they depended on to get them through the battle (like Trooper Assault) can really give you the advantage in any situation.

Sense and Alter

By: Maarten Logghe

Sense and Alter are always a threat in tournament play. The cards have the potential to ruin every deck. When you create a tournament deck, you should always think how you will protect yourself against SAC. It actually happens quite early in the deckbuilding process. You decide first with a general theme and then decide what to do about SAC. SAC has a big influence on deckbuilding.

If you decide SAC yourself, there's not that much of a guideline, except more is better. Especially pre-SE this was very much true. If you encountered someone else who also chose the SAC approach you had to have more of them than your opponent. Actually you need more in hand than your opponent, which is slightly different. (eg early light Tatooine decks could go with slightless SAC than dark tatooine decks. Because of Obi'hut and Obi for deploy of 2 and the overall lower deploy cost of the main characters. LS could draw more cards in hand, so increasing the chance of holding more SAC in hand.)

Monnok/grimtaasch is also essential in a SAC war. It forces your opponent to engage a SAC war, where the player of the Grimnok has the advantage (since you need only as much SAC as the opponent to win)

Let's take a closer look at the anti-SAC strategies and it's history by going back to the releases of expansions and what they changed.

Premiere

Sac could be only countered by Sac or by playing only non-red cards. Unfortunately that last thing was not much of an option back in those days. So Sac was played by all.

ANH

Introduced the grapples and grimtaasch/monnok, as an added defense. My favourite combo in those days was Tentacle/alter. I played with 2 Tentacles and about 4-5 Alters. 1 Tentacle + 1 alter and 1 force left, was almost foolproof. It was quite adequate. With the introduction of control in Dagobah, I abandoned the strategy. Control could outright cancel the alter.

Hoth

Nothing in this set that changed the environment.. except 'hardware' decks (decks with very little sense/alter vulnerable cards in them) became tournament viable mainly due to the introduction of walkers. There were enough cancel cards in non-interupt form who could cancel popular strategies. (eg Lt Pol Treidum, Seekers both to kill undercover spies). From Hoth on, hardware decks remained a viable strategy.

Dagobah

Introduced control. A very good card, cancels sense/alter and as secundary use cancels a force drain. + introduction of Do or do not and There's no try.

With Dagobah I think SAC was never more popular. Control was just an extra card in the SAC decks that could help in a SAC war and also cancel force drains.

Do or do not and there's no try, didn't have quite an effect, first of all, because you needed more than one in a deck to get one out before it was too late and second, because playing with Do or do not usually meant the opponent had no SAC and canceling a card was worth sacrificing 3 cards

for.

CC

Nothing...

Jabba's Palace

Starting interupts. now, you could start with that Do or do not, or There's no try. It made sure you had SAC protection from the start and you didn't need more than 1 copy. But the SAC player still had the advantage, he only needed to be careful what to sense/alter. But the opponent's crucial cards still went down.

Until here, in my opinion, the only viable SAC defense was playing a couple of 'push' cards (what are you trying to push on us and there'll be hell to pay) and controls. It was always difficult to play because of the constant threat of monnok/grimtaasch, killing your controls. The hardware option still remains the easiest way to play anti-SAC, unfortunately the deck itself had so many weaknesses because of the lack of red cards. But still in the hands of an excellent player, it was tournament viable.

SE

With the release of SE, there are a lot more possibilities... let's go past the cards which have an impact on the anti-SAC play and in the same time go past every anti-SAC possibility :

1) hardware deck

Still a viable deck, though with the recent escalation of power, a walker with 2 pilots isn't as safe anymore (especially with mechanical failure but I don't see it played very much). A good deck I see is Raltiir operations with no sense/alterable cards in.

2) HDADTJ/MWYHL

These cards have a huge impact. Not anymore has the opponent a good chance of canceling your card and paying for it, he isn't sure anymore. The flip side is of course the ultimate SAC counter. never rely on this objective alone to counter SAC. With the front side on, there's no discouragement whatsoever for the opponent to play his SAC. He still can play it, it always goes to the used pile and maybe it can cancel something! Use it in conjunction with either there's no try or what're you trying to push on us. My favourite start for these objectives is oppressive enforcement/wise advice. Not only because the go-to-used-pile function of canceled cards, but because of your pushcards deploying for free. Wise Advice has of course the added benefit of adding one to training destiny.

3) Wise Advice/oppressive enforcement

Don't rely on them as anti-SAC protection. Never play it alone that's not good enough. Your opponent can still cancel everything at will. In fact I only play this card when I can use the other functions of it too. eg in a deck with Stardestroyers for the dark side, or in a training deck for the light side.

4)Don't thread on me/Surface, Surface Defense

I like these cards. If I can start with it, I do. Sometimes I even use it as my only SAC protection. I only do this when I use a lot of interupts/effects. I prefer the nasty ones that keep returning. eg visored vision, sabac, narow escape, hyper escape, multiple on the edges, off the edges... and so on. that way there are too many of them to sense them all, and they are all sort of critical...

5) What're you trying to push on us/there'll be hell to pay

These cards are great anti-SAC. Put 2 of them in your deck and use it with 2 or 3 controls. Or use it with wise advice/oppressive enforcement...

6)Dark Forces/For Luck

These Jedi Pack cards let you exclude the highest ability character when drawing destiny for SAC. If you combine this with HDDTJ/MWYHL then there's no way the opponent can sense alter something. the only drawback to this card is you need 1 force. But sometimes leaving force is good all together, just so you won't be surprised by a draw their fire. Secundary use for this card is adding one destiny when your opponent plays suprise assault. With the abundance of Suprise assault/counter assault these days, this card might be a good choice.

Conclusion :

There are a wide variety of possibilities when deciding playing SAC or Anti-SAC much more than it used to play. All the better I would say. Since SE, Alters have become sometimes more critical than senses. All the good effects in SE made it really worthwile to stock some alters. So you could try to stock your deck with 4 alters and 2 push cards, especially if your area is low in SAC. It gives you sense protection (push the sense card and alter them afterwards), meanwhile giving you also an offense by canceling critical effects. (but be wary of monnok/grimtaasch)

my most popular anti-SAC combo's (I most of the time play without SAC)

2)Dark Alters + there'll be hell to pays (imo there are more nasty light used interupts so 2 there'll be hell to pays are needed in any dark deck)

3) MWYHL/HDDTJ + wise Advice/there's no try + push cards

4) MWYHL/HDDTJ + there's no try/do or do not

5) 2 pushcards + 3 controls

6) Hardware deck

7) MWYHAL/HDDTJ + For Luck

Traffic ControlRules.

1. This is top secret. This is my Regional, and (haha) WC deck.

2. Report any changes, wins, losses to me (include reason for change, and type of deck beat, or lost to.

3. HAVE FUN. This is a fun deck, as far as battling and opponent reactions go.

Ok, if Sense, Alter and Control are important to you, place Yoda in and remove two Frozen Assets, and a Sorry About The Mess. You must have Yoda in, and even then, it is only a 50-50 chance that you will succeed. Let me run, card by card, an analysis of what is in this deck and why it is there.

Mind What You Have Learned/Dagobah stuff - You will never use this. This is just to turbo the beginning, as well as to goad them into throwing down a bad Starting Effect. You will have at least six generation on turn two.

Forest - A good side strategy, if they play space. You can drop this down, and toss an EPP there, and you have immunity, and a Force Drain. This card is expendable, however.

Tatooine: Cantina, Yoda' s Hut - The main weakness of this deck, is the rare all out space deck. These give you a good drain, and a lure to come down. Revolution the Hut.

Jabba's Palace: Audience Chamber - I Must Be Allowed to Speak goes here, and when Luke comes down there, he is immune to attrition. Don't hold Luke waiting for it, though, you have 2, just lose the old, and drop the new.

Lars' Moisture Farm - Well, you kind of need this to run the Harvest effectively. Don't panic, the Lars retrieve 2 at any other site together, so you can get this back.

EPP's - These are for a quick run, just drop, slash, and forfeit. You get them back. Try to get Luke protected with I Must Be Allowed To Speak.

Ben Kenobi - Save Yoda's Gimer Stick for Ben, since you park him at Lars' Moisture Farm, with The Lars. Amazingly effective, the only characters who can battle here, are those with ability 4 or more - not a lot of those around. With the destiny of this deck ,Vader is easy meat. Lose A Lars, bring 'em back. Toss the villain out of play with Out Of Commission.

The Lars - 3 should prevent Mosep from eliminating him.

WORD ON MOSEP - Kind of a threat. He comes down, he gets hacked, and sent out of play. No questions asked, no matter the cost. You'll get them back.

Wyron Serper - This is your pocket boy. If you see Carbon Chamber Testing, that's what he was made for. If Hunt Down And Destroy The Jedi is hurting you, just use this guy to kill the Visage of the Emporer. He's a spy. He's a rebel. What more can you ask for?

Red Leader in R1, Landing Claw - This is kind of a weak answer to the space threat, but at least you can stick him on something and cancel a drain. If they move, you can ask where they are going. You can detach in any phase, and reattach to a different ship in a control phase. Don't let them move you to a site they can't drain at!

Harvest - This is the money maker. There is a trick to this, very simple: When you are set up, you will use very little force, this is for Secret Plans, and obtaining Harvest. At the end of your turn, in your draw phase, draw until you get a Harvest. Immediately cash it in. Draw for another. Use this in the opponents control phase, and you cannot ever be Grimtaashed with two in hand. Use second Harvest to get the first Harvest, and three others.

Out Of Commission - This is the fun card. Play these as soon as you get them, they are used, and you don't want them in your hand when they run Monnok. Remember, red cards (Interrupts and Effects) can still be used, even if "out of play." You don't have to inform your opponent this and quite a few will think the unique effects and interrupts can't be played. I make it a point to point out after the game, that they can still be used. Let them sense this, with Mind What You Have Learned adding four to the draw, their chances are very slim.

Nabrun Leids - This is to get into an Ops area, if you are landlocked on Tatooine.

Sorry About The Mess - The sniper card. Use this to eliminate a "big gun" that you don't want to face in battle. (I.E. Darth Vader or Boba Fett)

Grimtaash - The usual "too many cards in your hand for your own good" trick

The Signal - This, I used to use with Wise Advise, but with the new opening, Sense/Alter are almost useless. I guess you have to pick which one you want to start with, depending on what you see to be their strategy.

Shocking Info - The best counter to Scanning Crew yet. I only wish I had room for more. Usually, where there's one, there's more, so they may assume that you've more than one.

Quite A Mercenary - Nothing could be more painful than getting "Merced" then watching them all go out of play via Out Of Commission.

Frozen Assets - One of the Key cards. With Draw Their Fire on the table, you can beatdown, and they cannot Ghhhk away the damage. This should be how you inflict most of your damage.

Yoda's Gimer Stick. With this on Ben, and Ben at the Farm, the Clinton family should be so lucky! Their own bodyguard.

I Must Be Allowed To Speak - Luke's version of Yoda's Gimer Stick. Just shake well, and follow the directions on the side of the package.

Lightsaber Proficiency - As if Power 5, Immunity to attrition wasn't enough, let's make him power 8!

Revolution - Use this on the Hut - Obi's, that is. Drain of three. Good magnet for a beatdown.

Draw Their Fire - Part of the Frozen Assets thing. Even gives you a cheesy retrieval for initiating battle (ed. note Cheesy? Incentive to battle is cheesy?!?!), which you should be doing lots. Be careful, if gives him a retrieval, if he initiates. But he won't right? No force to retrieve, nobody to fight with. Right? ;)

Traffic Control - Your generic recycler. Two would be nice to always get this guy in opening hand...

Your major weakness is space, and Secret Plans. You drain for three at the hut, one at the farm, one at the forest, one at the Cantina, one at the Audience Chamber, for a total of eight. You retrieve eight for his round of Force Drain so, he must drain for 17, before you start losing the drain war. When retrieving, retrieve the last Harvest, and three high destiny, or if in peril, some EPP's. The trickiest part, is when to retrieve sites. You may need some for added Force Drain, or to boost differential. One neat trick, is to slowly bleed your forces off the table, into the lost pile, and back into the deck. VERY hard to do, and do well. you could however, get a differential of up to fifty if you did this.

Well, that is all, let me know of any cool tricks you come up with, and your successes. There should not be any failures! :) Let me know if you have any trouble assembling this deck, and I will help you finish.
